An executive once told this story. His company hired a consulting firm to build an ML model that would forecast how many of each grocery item to restock each week. The firm spent six months on it; the company deployed it, loved the results, and proudly told investors they were now "AI-powered." A year later, the numbers quietly fell apart — some items were chronically over-ordered and expired on the shelf, others under-ordered and lost sales. The inventory team hand-patched the predictions for a while, until the model drifted so far they gave up on it.

The lesson the whole industry is learning the hard way: **deploying a model is not the finish line.** A model's accuracy decays in production, often silently. Once it's live, you have to keep watching it to catch problems, and keep shipping updates to fix them.

This chapter covers why models that shine in development fail in production. We start with the general causes of ML system failures, then zoom in on the single thorniest one — **data distribution shifts**, when production data drifts away from what the model trained on. We finish with how to **monitor** a system so you can detect these shifts. (Chapter 9 covers how to *update* models to adapt to them.)

::: callout note "What counts as an ML system failure?"
A **failure** is a violation of an expectation. Traditional software has mostly *operational* expectations — latency, throughput, no crashes. An ML system has those **plus ML performance expectations**. For an English→French translator, "returns a translation within 1 s" is operational; "the translation is correct 99% of the time" is an ML expectation. Operational breakages announce themselves (timeouts, 404s, segfaults). ML failures **fail silently** — if you don't know the correct answer, you can't tell the model is wrong. That's why ML systems need active monitoring.
:::

### Software System Failures

These are failures that have nothing to do with ML — they'd happen to any software system. Common modes:

- **Dependency failure.** A package or service you rely on breaks, taking your system down with it. This is especially painful when a *third party* maintains the dependency — and worse if that third party no longer exists. (It's a big reason companies prefer open source: if the maintainer disappears, you can at least fork and maintain the code yourself.)
- **Deployment failure.** A mistake at deploy time — you accidentally ship the binaries of an older model version, or the new process lacks permission to read/write the files it needs.
- **Hardware failure.** The CPUs or GPUs you deploy on misbehave — overheating and dying, for example.
- **Downtime or crashing.** A component runs on a server (AWS, a hosted service) and that server goes down, so your system goes down too.

::: callout key "Most \"ML\" failures are really ops failures"
In 2020, two Google ML engineers (Papasian and Underwood) reviewed **96** outages of a large ML pipeline over 15 years. **60 of the 96** had causes *not directly related to ML* — mostly distributed-systems issues (a scheduler or orchestrator slipping up) and data-pipeline issues (sources joined incorrectly, wrong data structures). The punchline: **ML engineering is mostly engineering, not ML.** Fixing these failures needs solid software-engineering skills, not modeling skills.
:::

Why are software-system failures so common in ML right now? Because ML production tooling is still young — best practices aren't standardized yet. As the tooling matures, expect the share of software-system failures to **shrink** and the share of ML-specific failures to **grow**. That's why the rest of this chapter focuses on the ML-specific kind: they're rarer but far nastier.

### ML-Specific Failures

ML-specific failures are unique to ML systems: data-collection and processing problems, poor hyperparameters, training/inference pipelines that drift out of sync, distribution shifts, edge cases, and degenerate feedback loops. Earlier chapters covered data problems (Ch. 4), hyperparameter tuning (Ch. 6), and the danger of split train/inference pipelines (Ch. 7). Here we tackle three that surface *after* deployment.

#### Production data differing from training data

When a model "learns," it's learning the **underlying distribution** of its training data, hoping that distribution also describes the **unseen data** it'll meet in production. Your test set is a stand-in for unseen data — that's why test performance is supposed to predict real-world performance. The whole arrangement rests on one assumption: **training and unseen data come from the same (stationary) distribution.** This assumption is wrong most of the time, for two reasons.

**First, real-world data rarely matches the training set.** Curating a dataset that faithfully represents production is genuinely hard. Real-world data is multifaceted and effectively infinite; training data is finite, shaped by your time, compute, and labeling budget, and riddled with the selection and sampling biases from Chapter 4. The mismatch can be tiny — production encoding emojis differently than training — yet still produce the classic **train-serving skew**: a model that aces development but flops in production.

**Second, the world isn't stationary — it moves.** In 2019, people searching "Wuhan" wanted travel tips; after COVID-19, they wanted news about the pandemic's origin. So a second failure mode is a model that launches great, then degrades as the data shifts under it. This has to be monitored for the entire time the model is deployed.

::: callout warning "Don't blame the world too quickly"
COVID makes a dramatic example, but shifts aren't rare freak events — they happen **constantly**: suddenly (a competitor changes prices, you launch in a new region, a celebrity name-drops your product), gradually (cultures, slang, and trends drift), or seasonally (more rideshare requests on cold snowy nights). And beware: one monitoring-vendor CTO estimated **~80%** of the "drifts" his service flags are actually **internal bugs** — broken pipelines, mis-imputed missing values, train/inference feature mismatches, the wrong model version. Detecting a shift is hard; finding its *cause* is harder.
:::

#### Edge cases

Imagine a self-driving car that drives you safely 99.99% of the time, but the other 0.01% gets into a catastrophic, possibly fatal crash. Would you ride in it? Most people say no — which is exactly why a model that's excellent on average can still be **unusable** if its rare failures are catastrophic.

**Edge cases** are samples so extreme they make the model make catastrophic mistakes. They matter most in safety-critical domains (self-driving, medical diagnosis, traffic control, e-discovery), but also anywhere a single bad output is unacceptable — a support chatbot that's pleasant 99% of the time but occasionally spews racist content is a brand risk no company can ship.

::: callout note "Outlier vs edge case — not the same thing"
An **outlier** is about the *data*: an example far from the others. An **edge case** is about *performance*: an example where the model does far worse than usual. An outlier can *cause* an edge case, but needn't — a person jaywalking on a highway is an outlier, yet not an edge case if the car detects them and responds correctly. Note the asymmetry: in **development** you can drop outliers to learn cleaner decision boundaries, but at **inference** you can't refuse the weird queries — you must build a model that survives unexpected inputs. And a sudden spike in edge cases is itself a clue that the **underlying distribution has shifted**.
:::

#### Degenerate feedback loops

Recall the *feedback loop* from Chapter 4: the time between showing a prediction and getting feedback on it (which becomes a natural label for the next model). A **degenerate feedback loop** arises when the predictions *themselves* shape that feedback — when a **system's outputs become its own future inputs**, which then bias its future outputs. These loops are rampant in tasks with user-generated labels: recommender systems and click-through-rate prediction.

Concretely: a song recommender ranks A a hair above B. Because A shows up higher, users click it more, which convinces the model A is great, so it ranks A even higher — and soon A buries B. This is why popular songs, books, and movies keep getting more popular while new items struggle to break in. The phenomenon is heavily researched under many names: **exposure bias, popularity bias, filter bubbles, echo chambers.**

A darker example: a résumé screener finds feature X ("went to Stanford," "worked at Google," or "identifies as male") predicts qualification. Recruiters only interview model-recommended candidates → only candidates with X → only hires with X → the model weights X even more. Left unchecked, degenerate loops don't just hurt accuracy; they **amplify and entrench bias.**

**Detecting degenerate feedback loops.** Offline, they're hard to spot — there are no users yet. But for recommenders you can measure the **popularity diversity** of outputs even offline. Item popularity follows a **long-tail distribution** (a few items dominate; most are rarely touched). Metrics like *aggregate diversity* and *average coverage of long-tail items* flag homogeneous outputs. Going further, Chia et al. (2021) bucket items by popularity and measure prediction accuracy per bucket — if the model is far better at recommending popular items than rare ones, it likely suffers **popularity bias**. And once in production, if outputs grow **more homogeneous over time**, that's the loop tightening.

**Correcting degenerate feedback loops.** Two practical fixes:

| Method | Idea | Cost / refinement |
|---|---|---|
| **Randomization** | Don't show only top-ranked items — inject random ones and use their feedback to gauge true quality. TikTok gives each new video a random initial traffic pool to measure unbiased quality before promoting it. | Improves diversity but hurts UX (fully random items annoy users). Use an **intelligent exploration strategy** like contextual bandits, or a *small* dose of randomization + causal inference (Schnabel et al.) to keep accuracy. |
| **Positional features** | Feedback is biased by *where* a prediction appears. Encode position as a feature (numerical `1,2,3…` or boolean "shown first?") so the model learns how much being on top drives clicks. | At inference, set "1st position" = False to predict click probability independent of slot. A stronger two-model version: model 1 predicts whether the user *considers* an item given its position; model 2 predicts click *given* consideration (position-free). |

::: callout example "Positional feature in a training row"
You add a `1st_Position` column to the song-recommendation training data: `(Shallow, Pop, Lady Gaga, 1st_Position=True → Click=Yes)` vs the same song shown lower → `Click=No`. The model can now separate "users love this song" from "users click whatever is on top." Note: positional **features** are different from the positional **embeddings** of Chapter 5.
:::

## Data Distribution Shifts

Now the headline failure. **Data distribution shift** is the supervised-learning phenomenon where the data a model sees changes over time, eroding its accuracy. The data the model trained on is the **source distribution**; the data it runs inference on is the **target distribution**. (The idea isn't new — it's been studied since at least 1986 — but it's only gone mainstream as ML enters industry.)

### Types of Data Distribution Shifts

The terms *concept drift*, *covariate shift*, and *label shift* are often blurred together, but they're three distinct subtypes. This part is math-heavy and mostly matters from a research angle — in production, practitioners rarely stop to classify a shift; they just want to handle it. If the notation gets dense, skip to *General Data Distribution Shifts*.

Let $X$ be the model's inputs and $Y$ its outputs. Training data is a sample from the **joint distribution** $P(X, Y)$, and ML usually models $P(Y \mid X)$. The joint distribution decomposes two ways:

$$P(X, Y) = P(Y \mid X)\,P(X) \qquad P(X, Y) = P(X \mid Y)\,P(Y)$$

Reading the pieces: $P(Y \mid X)$ is the probability of an output given an input (e.g., spam given the email's content), $P(X)$ is the input density, and $P(Y)$ is the output density. The three shift types each freeze one piece and move another:

| Shift | What changes | What stays fixed | One-line intuition |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Covariate shift** | $P(X)$ | $P(Y \mid X)$ | Inputs look different; the input→output rule is unchanged |
| **Label shift** | $P(Y)$ | $P(X \mid Y)$ | The mix of outputs changes; per-output inputs unchanged |
| **Concept drift** | $P(Y \mid X)$ | $P(X)$ | Same inputs, different outputs ("posterior shift") |

#### Covariate shift

A *covariate* is an independent variable that influences an outcome but isn't itself the thing you care about (running a housing-price study, square footage is a covariate). In supervised ML, the label is the variable of interest and the **input features are the covariates** — so **covariate shift means $P(X)$ moves while $P(Y \mid X)$ holds.**

Example: detecting breast cancer with age as a feature. Risk rises after 40, and your training data has more over-40 women than your inference data — so the **input distributions differ.** But for a given age, the probability of cancer is unchanged: $P(\text{cancer} \mid \text{age}>40)$ is constant. That's covariate shift.

In **development**, covariate shift creeps in through biased selection (a clinic where mostly over-40s show up → *sample selection bias*), through deliberately altered training data (oversampling rare classes to fight imbalance), or through **active learning** (picking the most informative samples warps the input distribution away from reality). In **production**, it usually comes from a real change in how the app is used — a marketing campaign attracts a more affluent demographic, so your "predict free→paid conversion" model sees a new income distribution, even though the conversion probability *given* income is unchanged. If you somehow know in advance how inputs will differ, **importance weighting** helps: estimate the density ratio between real-world and training inputs, weight the training data by it, and retrain. In practice you rarely know the future shift, so robustly pre-training for it is very hard.

#### Label shift

**Label shift** (a.k.a. prior shift, prior probability shift, target shift) is when **$P(Y)$ changes but $P(X \mid Y)$ stays fixed** — the output mix changes, but for a given output the inputs look the same.

Covariate and label shift often travel together. In the breast-cancer example, more over-40s in training means more POSITIVE labels there too, so $P(Y)$ shifts — yet pick a cancer patient from training and one from test, and both are equally likely to be over 40, so $P(X \mid Y)$ holds. That's *both* shifts at once. But not every covariate shift causes label shift: introduce a preventive drug that lowers everyone's cancer risk, and $P(Y \mid X)$ drops for all ages (no longer covariate shift), yet the age profile of cancer patients is unchanged — still label shift. Because the two are closely related, the methods to detect and adapt to them are similar.

#### Concept drift

**Concept drift** (a.k.a. posterior shift) is **"same input, different output"**: $P(Y \mid X)$ changes while $P(X)$ holds. A house-price model is the classic case — pre-COVID a three-bedroom SF apartment cost \$2,000,000; early-COVID, as people fled the city, the *same* apartment cost \$1,500,000. The feature distribution didn't move, but the price *given* those features did.

Concept drift is often **cyclic or seasonal** — rideshare prices swing weekday vs weekend, flight prices spike over holidays. A common response is to keep **separate models** for each regime (one for weekdays, one for weekends).

### General Data Distribution Shifts

Beyond the three textbook types, other real-world changes degrade models even though research barely studies them:

- **Feature change.** New features added, old ones removed, or a feature's set of possible values changes — e.g., the `age` feature switches from years to months, drifting its range. (One team's model cratered when a pipeline bug turned a feature into all `NaN`s.)
- **Label schema change.** The set of possible $Y$ values changes — so **both $P(Y)$ and $P(X \mid Y)$ change** (unlike label shift, where only $P(Y)$ moves). For **regression**, the value range changes (a credit score moving from 300–850 to 250–900). For **classification**, classes are added, retired, or split — marketing wants the NEGATIVE sentiment class broken into SAD and ANGRY, taking you from 3 classes to 4. Changing the class count can change the **model's structure** (a softmax layer's size) and force you to **relabel and retrain from scratch.** This is especially common in high-cardinality tasks like product or document categorization.

And there's no rule that only one shift happens at a time — a model can suffer **several at once**, which makes them much harder to untangle.

### Detecting Data Distribution Shifts

A shift only matters if it **hurts performance**, so the first instinct is to watch **accuracy-related metrics** — accuracy, F1, recall, AUC-ROC — in production. "Change" usually means "decrease," but a sudden, unexplained *jump* or wild fluctuation is worth investigating too.

The catch: accuracy metrics need **ground-truth labels**, and in production you often don't have them — or they arrive heavily delayed (recall *Natural Labels*, Ch. 4). When labels are missing or too late, you instead monitor the **distributions** themselves: the input $P(X)$, the label $P(Y)$, and the conditionals $P(X \mid Y)$ and $P(Y \mid X)$. You can watch $P(X)$ **without** knowing $Y$, but the others all require $Y$. Research has tried to detect label shift without target labels (Black Box Shift Estimation, Lipton et al. 2018), but in industry, **most drift detection focuses on the input distribution — especially feature distributions.**

#### Statistical methods

The simplest approach many companies use: compare **summary statistics** — min, max, mean, median, variance, quantiles (5th/25th/75th/95th), skewness, kurtosis — between training and inference. (As of late 2021, even TensorFlow Extended's data-validation tools relied only on summary stats.) It's a fine *start*, but weak: these stats only summarize distributions for which they're meaningful, and crucially, **if the stats match, that does NOT guarantee there's no shift** — only a mismatch is informative.

A stronger tool is a **two-sample hypothesis test**: does the difference between two sets of data rise above sampling noise? Treat **yesterday** as the source population and **today** as the target; if they're statistically different, the distribution likely shifted overnight.

::: callout key "Statistically significant ≠ practically important"
A handy heuristic: if you can detect the difference from a **small** sample, it's probably a *serious* difference worth acting on. If it takes a **huge** sample to surface, the shift is probably too minor to worry about.
:::

The go-to basic test is the **Kolmogorov–Smirnov (KS) test** — nonparametric, so it assumes nothing about the underlying distribution and works for any of them. Its big limitation: **KS only handles one-dimensional data.** That's fine for scalar predictions or labels, but features are usually high-dimensional, where KS doesn't apply (and can be expensive and trigger false positives). Alternatives include **Least-Squares Density Difference** and **MMD (Maximum Mean Discrepancy)** plus its Learned-Kernel variant — popular in research, rare in industry. Since two-sample tests work better on low-dimensional data, **reduce dimensionality before testing.** (The open-source **Alibi Detect** package implements many of these.)

#### Time-scale windows: why temporal shifts are hard

Not all shifts are equal — **abrupt** changes are easy to catch; **gradual** ones are slow to surface. Shifts span two dimensions: **spatial** (a new group of users, a new device type) and **temporal** (change over time). For temporal shifts, the standard move is to treat the input stream as **time-series data** — and then the **time-scale window you choose decides what you can even see.**

The window matters because **seasonality confounds everything.** If your data has a weekly cycle, a window shorter than a week can't even see the cycle. Take the figure above: with days **9–14** as the reference, day 15 looks like a shift — but with days **1–14** as reference, day 15 is just the normal weekly pattern. Detecting temporal shifts is genuinely hard when seasonal variation is in the mix.

::: callout note "Sliding vs cumulative statistics"
**Sliding** stats are computed within one window (say, an hour) and **reset** each window. **Cumulative** stats keep accumulating across windows and never reset. Because cumulative stats carry the past with them, they can **mask** what's happening right now — a cumulative accuracy curve can completely hide a sharp dip between hours 16 and 18. For catching sudden problems, prefer sliding windows.
:::

In practice, most companies use the **training distribution as the base** and monitor production at a fixed granularity (hourly, daily). Shorter windows detect change faster but raise more false alarms. Some platforms offer a **merge** operation (roll hourly stats up into daily views) and even **root-cause analysis** that scans many window sizes to pinpoint exactly when the change happened. Working in the temporal space pulls in time-series techniques (like seasonal decomposition) that are beyond this book's scope — and worth reiterating: not every degradation needs an ML fix, since many shifts are really **human errors** you must find first.

### Addressing Data Distribution Shifts

How teams respond depends on infrastructure maturity. Beginners may not feel the pain until a model has quietly rotted for three-to-six months; more mature teams assume shift is inevitable and **retrain on a schedule** (daily/weekly/monthly) regardless of measured drift. The *optimal* retraining frequency is an important decision most companies still make on gut feel rather than experiment (more in Ch. 9).

There are three broad ways to make a model work on a new distribution:

1. **Train on a massive dataset** (today's research default) — make the training distribution so comprehensive that production data is probably a subset of it.
2. **Adapt a trained model without new labels** — e.g., causal/kernel methods (Zhang et al. 2013) or domain-invariant representation learning (Zhao et al. 2020) that correct for shift without target labels. Promising but underexplored; little industry adoption.
3. **Retrain on labeled data from the target distribution** (what industry actually does). But "retrain" hides two real decisions:

| Decision | Option A | Option B |
|---|---|---|
| **From where?** | **Stateless retraining** — train from scratch on old + new data | **Stateful training (fine-tuning)** — continue from the last checkpoint on new data |
| **On what data?** | Last 24 h / last week / last 6 months | From the point the data began drifting |

You'll often need experiments to find the best combination (covered in Ch. 9). (Aside: this is closely related to **domain adaptation** — treat each distribution as a domain — and **transfer learning** — treat each joint distribution $P(X,Y)$ as a task; the difference is that adapting to a new distribution may require retraining from scratch, whereas transfer learning reuses the base model.)

::: callout tip "Design for robustness — don't just react"
You can make a system **resilient to shift up front** by choosing stable features. A model predicting app downloads *could* use the app's store ranking — but rankings swing wildly, forcing constant retraining. Bucketing rank into coarse bands (top 10, 11–100, 101–1,000…) is far more stable, though less predictive. There's a real **performance-vs-stability trade-off**: a sharp but fast-decaying feature drags you into frequent retraining. You can also **split models by regime** — house prices move faster in San Francisco than rural Arizona, so separate models let you update each only when *it* needs it, instead of retraining one shared model at SF's pace.
:::

**Monitoring** tracks, measures, and logs metrics to tell you *when* something goes wrong. **Observability** sets up the system — **instrumentation** — so you have the visibility to investigate *why* it went wrong, without shipping new code to probe it. Instrumentation means adding timers to functions, counting NaNs in features, tracing how inputs transform, logging unusual events. **Observability is part of monitoring** — without some observability, monitoring is impossible.

Monitoring starts with **operational metrics** — the health of the system itself, at three levels: **network**, **machine**, and **application**. Think latency, throughput, request counts per minute/hour/day, the % of requests returning 2xx, CPU/GPU and memory utilization. No matter how good the model, if the system is down you get nothing from it.

::: callout example "Availability, uptime, and SLAs"
**Availability** = how often the system is up offering reasonable performance, measured as **uptime**. The bar is set in an **SLO** (service level objective) or **SLA** (service level agreement) — e.g., "up = median latency under 200 ms *and* p99 under 2 s." A provider's SLA might promise **99.99%** uptime (four nines), refunding credits if missed. The arithmetic is brutal: 99.99% allows only ~4 minutes of downtime per month; **99.999%** allows just **26 seconds**.
:::

### ML-Specific Metrics

But for ML, "up" isn't enough — a live system serving garbage predictions still fails its users. ML-specific metrics track the **health of the model**, across four artifacts produced at successive pipeline stages. There's a tradeoff along the pipeline: the **deeper** the artifact, the more transformations it's passed through — so a change is more likely caused by a *transformation bug*, but the artifact is also more **structured** and closer to what you care about, hence easier to monitor.

- **Accuracy-related metrics.** The most *direct* signal of degradation. Log every scrap of user feedback — clicks, hides, purchases, up/downvotes, shares — since some becomes natural labels. Even feedback that *isn't* a direct label can reveal trouble: if a video recommender's **click-through rate** holds but **completion rate** falls, the recommendations are getting worse. You can also *engineer* feedback collection (Google Translate's up/down vote buttons), using a spike in downvotes both as an alarm and to guide relabeling.
- **Predictions.** The most common artifact to monitor, and the easiest — each prediction is essentially one number (low-dimensional), so it's cheap to visualize and to run two-sample tests on. Prediction shifts are also a **proxy for input shifts**: if the model's weights haven't changed, a moved prediction distribution implies a moved input distribution. And odd patterns surface fast — a model emitting all-`False` for 10 minutes is caught immediately, long before delayed labels would reveal it.
- **Features.** Where most industry monitoring focuses, because features are **well-structured** under a schema. **Feature validation** ("unit tests for data," a.k.a. table testing) checks expectations: is min/max/median in range? does it match a regex? is every value in a known set? is feature A always greater than feature B? Open-source helpers include **Great Expectations** and AWS's **Deequ**. You can also run two-sample tests on features, reducing dimensionality first.
- **Raw inputs.** Hardest to monitor — they arrive from many sources in many formats, and ML engineers usually can't even access them directly (a data-platform team owns and pre-processes them). So this is typically the **data platform team's** responsibility, and out of scope here.

::: callout warning "Four headaches of feature monitoring"
1. **Compute/memory cost** — hundreds of models × thousands of features; even hourly summary stats get expensive and can slow the system, *raising* latency and detection time.
2. **Weak link to performance** — most feature drifts are **benign**; alerting on every one breeds **alert fatigue**, where the team tunes out. The real problem becomes deciding which shifts are *critical*.
3. **Multi-step extraction** — features are built across many steps, libraries (pandas, Spark), and services (BigQuery, Snowflake), so even a real change is hard to trace to a data shift vs a processing bug.
4. **Evolving schema** — without versioned schemas mapped to features, an alert may just mean a schema mismatch, not a data change.
:::

### Monitoring Toolbox

The industry often cites "metrics, logs, and traces" as the three pillars — but from the *user's* perspective it's cleaner to think in **logs, dashboards, and alerts.**

**Logs.** Record runtime events: container starts, memory use, function calls and their inputs/outputs, crashes, stack traces, error codes. As Etsy's Ian Malpass put it, *"If it moves, we track it"* (and some things that haven't moved yet, in case they do). Volume explodes fast — the dating app Badoo handled **20 billion events a day** in 2019 — so when something breaks, finding the cause is a needle-in-a-haystack search. The hard part today isn't *when* something happened but **where**: a request may take 20–30 hops across containers, microservices, and serverless functions. **Distributed tracing** addresses this — give each process a unique ID and tag every event with metadata (time, service, function, user) so you can later reconstruct the path. Logs can be processed in **batch** (periodic SQL/Spark queries — efficient, but you only find problems periodically) or as a **stream** (Kafka/Kinesis transport + streaming SQL like KSQL or Flink SQL) to catch anomalies the moment they're logged. Many companies even apply ML to logs — anomaly detection, event prioritization, predicting which downstream services a failure will hit.

**Dashboards.** A picture beats a column of numbers, and dashboards make metrics legible to **non-engineers** (PMs, business stakeholders) too. But graphs alone aren't enough — you still need statistical sense to tell a meaningful shift from a wiggly line. And piling on metrics backfires: **dashboard rot** sets in, so pick the right metrics or roll low-level ones up into higher-level signals.

**Alerts.** When monitoring spots something suspicious, the right people must hear about it. An alert has three parts:

| Component | What it specifies | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Alert policy** | The condition (often a threshold over a duration) | "accuracy under 90%," or "latency over 1 s for ≥ 10 min" |
| **Notification channels** | Who gets told, and where | email `mlops-monitoring@…`, Slack `#mlops-monitoring`, PagerDuty |
| **Description** | Enough context to act | timestamp, service name, and a **runbook** of mitigation steps |

::: callout warning "Alert fatigue is dangerous, not just annoying"
Being woken at 3 a.m. for something outside your remit is demoralizing — and worse, a flood of trivial alerts **desensitizes** people to the critical ones. Set meaningful conditions so only alerts that matter ever fire.
:::

### Observability

Since the mid-2010s the industry has leaned toward **observability** over plain monitoring. The difference is an assumption. **Monitoring** makes no assumption linking a system's internal state to its outputs — you watch external outputs and hope they reveal internal trouble. **Observability** makes the *stronger* assumption that **internal state can be inferred from external outputs** — a concept borrowed from control theory.

Why the shift? Old systems were simple: a few components, one team owning the whole codebase, so you could poke the system to find a bug. Today's systems sprawl across many components, many of them **third-party cloud services** you don't control. You can't break the system apart to look inside — you must reason from its **telemetry** (logs and metrics collected at runtime; *tele* = remote, *metron* = measure).

The payoff: with an observable system, when something breaks you diagnose it from existing logs and metrics **without shipping new code.** Where monitoring deals in *aggregated* metrics, observability supports **fine-grained slicing** — not just "accuracy dropped," but *for which inputs, which user subgroups, over which window.* You should be able to query: *"show all users where model A was wrong in the last hour, grouped by zip code,"* or *"show outlier requests in the last 10 minutes,"* or *"show every intermediate output for this input."* That requires having **tagged** your outputs with identifying keywords up front.

::: callout key "Observability includes interpretability"
**Interpretability** explains how the *model* works; **observability** explains how the whole *system* (model included) works. When performance dips, being able to interpret *which feature* drove the wrong predictions over the last hour is what turns "something's broken" into "here's the fix." (Recall *Feature Importance*, Ch. 5.)
:::

One closing caveat: monitoring, for all its power, is **inherently passive** — you wait for a shift, then detect it. It surfaces problems without correcting them. Chapter 9 introduces **continual learning**, the paradigm for *actively* updating models to close the loop.

## Summary

This was, in the author's words, the hardest chapter to write — not because the ideas are abstruse, but because the literature is thin. Unusually for ML, this is an area where **research is still catching up to production.**

ML systems fail in two ways: **software-system failures** (the kind any system suffers — and still the *majority* of ML incidents today) and **ML-specific failures**. Among the latter, three big causes appear after deployment: **production data differing from training data**, **edge cases**, and **degenerate feedback loops** (the only one rooted in system *design*, since it's the model's own outputs poisoning its inputs).

The headline ML-specific failure is **data distribution shift**, in three formal flavors — **covariate** ($P(X)$ moves), **label** ($P(Y)$ moves), and **concept drift** ($P(Y \mid X)$ moves). The research narrative is still unsettled (the same phenomena go by different names), and most methods assume you know the future shift or have labels for both source and target — luxuries you rarely have in practice.

To detect shifts you must **monitor**, which is fundamentally about **metrics** — operational ones (latency, throughput, utilization) and ML-specific ones (accuracy, predictions, features, raw inputs). The hard part isn't *computing* metrics but *understanding* them: a wiggly dashboard line could be benign noise, a real shift, or a pipeline bug, and telling them apart takes statistical judgment. Detection is only step one — the next chapter is about **adapting** the system to a moving world.

## Key takeaways

::: takeaways
- A failure violates an expectation; ML systems have **operational** *and* **ML-performance** expectations, and ML failures often **fail silently**. Deploying a model is the start of its maintenance, not the end.
- **Most ML incidents are ordinary software-system failures** (dependencies, deployment, hardware, downtime) — ML engineering is mostly engineering. ML-specific failures are rarer but harder to detect and can sink a project.
- **Degenerate feedback loops** form when a model's predictions shape its own future training data (popularity/exposure bias). Detect via output homogeneity / hit-rate-by-popularity; correct with **randomization** or **positional features**.
- The three shift types: **covariate** ($P(X)$ changes, $P(Y\mid X)$ fixed), **label** ($P(Y)$ changes, $P(X\mid Y)$ fixed), **concept drift** ($P(Y\mid X)$ changes — "same input, different output").
- A shift only matters if accuracy drops. With labels, watch accuracy metrics; without, watch the **input distribution** using **two-sample tests** (e.g., **KS**, one-dimensional only) — reduce dimensionality first, and remember matching stats don't prove "no shift."
- **Time windows decide what you can detect**: seasonality confounds temporal shifts, and **cumulative** stats can hide a dip that **sliding** stats reveal.
- Address shifts by **retraining** — choose **stateless** (from scratch) vs **stateful** (fine-tune) and what data window — or design **robust, stable features** and split models by regime up front.
- **Monitoring** = track known metrics and alert on them (passive). **Observability** = instrument the system so you can ask new "why" questions after the fact, including **interpretability** to pin down the cause.
:::

```flashcards
Q: What does it mean that ML systems often "fail silently"?
A: An ML system can return wrong outputs while still meeting all *operational* expectations (no crash, no timeout). Since you usually don't know the correct answer in production, the **ML-performance** failure goes undetected unless you actively monitor model quality.
---
Q: A large Google study found what fraction of ML pipeline outages were NOT directly ML-related, and why does it matter?
A: 60 of 96 outages (≈⅔) were ordinary software/distributed-systems and data-pipeline failures. It shows **ML engineering is mostly engineering** — many "ML" failures need software skills, not modeling skills, to fix.
---
Q: What is a degenerate feedback loop, and name two ways to correct one.
A: It's when a model's **own predictions shape its future training data** (e.g., recommending an item gets it clicked more, which trains the model to recommend it even more — popularity/exposure bias). Fixes: **randomization** (show some random items to learn their true quality) and **positional features** (encode where a prediction was shown so the model separates quality from position).
---
Q: Define covariate, label, and concept shift in terms of P(X), P(Y), and P(Y|X).
A: **Covariate shift:** P(X) changes, P(Y|X) fixed (inputs move, mapping stays). **Label shift:** P(Y) changes, P(X|Y) fixed (output mix moves). **Concept drift:** P(Y|X) changes, P(X) fixed ("same input, different output").
---
Q: Why is the Kolmogorov–Smirnov (KS) test of limited use for monitoring features?
A: KS is nonparametric (no distribution assumptions) but works only on **one-dimensional** data. Features are usually high-dimensional, so you must reduce dimensionality first; KS can also be costly and prone to false positives.
---
Q: If a feature's production summary statistics (mean, median, variance) match the training data's, has the distribution definitely not shifted?
A: No. Matching summary stats do **not** guarantee there's no shift — only a *mismatch* is informative. Two different distributions can share the same mean/median/variance.
---
Q: What's the difference between monitoring and observability?
A: **Monitoring** tracks predefined metrics and alerts when they breach thresholds (passive — you wait for a known failure). **Observability** instruments the system so you can ask *new* questions after the fact ("why did this break?") from existing logs/telemetry, without shipping new code. Observability includes interpretability.
---
Q: Why can cumulative accuracy hide a real problem that sliding accuracy would catch?
A: Cumulative stats accumulate across all past windows and never reset, so they're dominated by history and can mask a sudden short-term dip. Sliding stats reset each window, exposing the dip in (say) hours 16–18.
```
