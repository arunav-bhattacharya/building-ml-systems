With the big picture from Chapter 1 in hand, we can move to the fun part: actually *designing* an ML system. Designing a system means taking the whole thing into account at once — the business reason it exists, the data stack, the infrastructure, the deployment story, the monitoring — and making sure every component and every stakeholder pulls toward the same objectives.

This chapter is the scaffolding for the rest of the book. We start with **objectives** (a project that can't explain *why* it exists gets killed). We set out four **requirements** that any decent ML system should satisfy. We sketch the **iterative process** you'll actually follow — a loop, not a straight line. We learn to **frame** a vague business request as a concrete ML task with inputs, outputs, and an objective function. And we close with the long-running debate that quietly shapes the whole field: do you win with a smarter **mind** or with more **data**?

::: callout note "Objectives vs. objective functions"
Watch the vocabulary in this chapter — three different things wear the word *objective*. A **business objective** is a company goal (more revenue, less churn). An **ML objective** is a model metric you optimize the project around (accuracy, F1, latency). An **objective function** (a.k.a. **loss function**) is the *mathematical* function a model minimizes while training. They're related but distinct, and we'll be precise about which one we mean.
:::

## Business and ML Objectives

When a data scientist starts an ML project, they tend to fixate on **ML metrics**: accuracy, F1 score, inference latency, log loss. They get a genuine thrill from nudging a model from **94% to 94.2%** accuracy, and will happily burn data, compute, and weeks of engineering time to get there.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **most companies don't care.** Nudging accuracy from 94% to 94.2% means nothing to a business unless it moves a **business metric**. The most common way ML projects die young is exactly this mismatch — the data scientists chase ML metrics while their manager watches business metrics, sees no connection between the two, and quietly kills the project (sometimes letting the team go with it).

So what *do* companies care about? According to the Nobel-winning economist Milton Friedman, the sole purpose of a business is to maximize profit for shareholders. Every project inside a business therefore exists to increase profit, either:

- **Directly** — more sales (higher conversion rates), or lower costs.
- **Indirectly** — higher customer satisfaction, more time spent on the site, and the like, which *eventually* convert to profit.

For an ML project to survive, you must tie the system's performance to overall business performance. Ask bluntly: *which business metric is this system supposed to move?* Ad revenue? Monthly active users? Purchase-through rate?

Some use cases make this mapping easy, which is exactly why they're so popular. For **ad click-through-rate prediction**, every extra click is real ad revenue. For **fraud detection**, every fraudulent transaction stopped is real money saved. The line from model to money is short and visible.

Many companies invent their own bridge metric. **Netflix** measures its recommender with **take-rate** — quality plays divided by recommendations shown. A higher take-rate, they found, also drives more total streaming hours and fewer cancellations, so a single ML-facing number connects cleanly to the business.

::: callout warning "The effect on business metrics can be genuinely hard to reason about"
The same personalization model that makes customers happier (so they spend *more*) might also solve their problems faster (so they spend *less*). And ML is often just one cog in a larger pipeline — in a cybersecurity system, an anomaly detector feeds an if-else rule set, which feeds human analysts, which feeds a takedown process. If a threat slips through, you may never know whether the ML part was to blame. The reliable way to settle "does this model move the business?" is **experiments** — A/B tests — and many companies pick the model with better *business* metrics even when its *ML* metrics are worse.
:::

A reality check on hype: ML returns rarely arrive overnight. Some firms imagine ML will magically transform the business by next quarter. *Magically — possible. Overnight — no.* Google has reaped enormous gains from ML, but only after investing for **decades**. Returns also compound with **maturity**: in a 2020 Algorithmia survey, ~75% of companies with models in production for over five years could deploy a new model in under 30 days, while ~60% of newcomers needed *more* than 30 days. The longer you've run ML, the cheaper and faster each new model gets.

## Requirements for ML Systems

You can't claim you've built a good ML system without saying what "good" requires. Exact requirements vary by use case, but four characteristics show up almost everywhere: **reliability, scalability, maintainability, and adaptability.**

### Reliability

The system should keep performing the **correct** function at the desired level of performance even under adversity — hardware faults, software bugs, human error.

The hard part for ML is that *correctness* is slippery. Your code can call `model.predict()` flawlessly and still return wrong predictions — and with no ground-truth labels at hand, you may have no way to *know* they're wrong. Traditional software fails loudly (a crash, a stack trace, a 404). ML systems can **fail silently**: the prediction is bad, but the user has no signal and keeps trusting it. If you use Google Translate into a language you don't speak, you simply can't tell when it's wrong.

### Scalability

An ML system can grow along **three** different axes, and each demands a sensible way to cope:

- **Complexity.** Last year a logistic regression fit in a 1 GB free-tier instance; this year a 100-million-parameter network needs 16 GB just to serve.
- **Traffic.** You launched serving 10,000 requests a day; as the user base grows, daily volume swings between 1 and 10 million.
- **Model count.** You start with one model, then add one to filter NSFW content and another to filter bot tweets. This is rampant in **enterprise**: one startup the author worked with ran **8,000 models in production — one per enterprise customer.**

Most people equate scalability with **resource scaling**: **up-scaling** (add machines to absorb a spike) and **down-scaling** (release them when idle). If peak needs 100 GPUs but the average is 10, keeping 100 running constantly is wasteful — so you scale down.

::: callout note "Scaling out is not scaling up — and autoscaling is harder than it looks"
**Up-scaling** and **down-scaling** are two sides of **scaling out**: adding (or removing) equivalent components in parallel to spread load. That's different from **scaling up**: making a single component bigger or faster. The cloud answer is **autoscaling** — adjusting machine count automatically with demand — but it's notoriously tricky. Even **Amazon** got bitten when autoscaling failed on Prime Day and the system crashed; an hour of downtime was estimated to cost between \$72 million and \$99 million.
:::

Scaling is not only about resources — it's also about **artifact management**. Managing 100 models is nothing like managing one. With a single model you might monitor and retrain it by hand, with one file to reproduce it. At 100 models, monitoring and retraining *must* be automated, and you need disciplined ways to generate and reproduce each model on demand.

### Maintainability

Many different people touch an ML system — ML engineers, DevOps engineers, subject-matter experts (SMEs) — from different backgrounds, with different tools, owning different slices. Set things up so each contributor can work with tools they're comfortable with, instead of one group forcing its stack on everyone else. Concretely: **document** the code; **version** code, data, and artifacts; make models **reproducible** enough that someone can build on them even after the original authors are gone. When something breaks, contributors should debug it together — no finger-pointing.

### Adaptability

To keep up with shifting data distributions and changing business needs, the system needs the capacity to (1) **discover** opportunities for improvement and (2) **update without service interruption**. Because an ML system is part code and part *data* — and data changes fast — it must evolve fast. Adaptability is tightly coupled to maintainability: a system that's painful to change is a system that won't adapt.

| Requirement | What it means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Reliability** | Keep doing the *correct* thing at the desired performance, even under faults — and beware **silent failures** | A translation model returns fluent-but-wrong output with no error raised |
| **Scalability** | Cope with growth in complexity, traffic, and model count via resource scaling **and** artifact management | Autoscale 10→100 GPUs at peak; manage 8,000 per-customer models |
| **Maintainability** | Let diverse contributors collaborate; document, version, and reproduce code/data/artifacts | An SME and an ML engineer debug the same pipeline without finger-pointing |
| **Adaptability** | Adapt to data/business shifts; update without downtime | Refresh a model on recent data when last month's distribution goes stale |

## Iterative Process

Building an ML system is **iterative** and, in most cases, **never-ending**: once a system is in production it must be continually monitored and updated. Newcomers often imagine a straight line — collect data, train, deploy, done. In reality it's a **cycle** with constant back-and-forth.

To see why, consider a real workflow for a model that decides whether to show an ad for a search query. You pick a metric (say, impressions); collect and label data; engineer features; train. During error analysis you discover the **labels are wrong**, so you relabel and retrain. You then notice the model *always* says "don't show the ad" — because 99.99% of your data has **negative** labels — so you collect more positive examples and retrain. The model does well on test data that's now **two months stale**, yet flops on yesterday's traffic, so you refresh it on recent data and retrain *again*. Finally you deploy — and the business comes knocking because revenue is *down*: ads are shown but nobody clicks. So you switch the objective to **click-through rate** and... go back to step 1. That endless looping is the point.

Each step gets its own deep dive later in the book; here's the brief tour:

- **Step 1 — Project scoping.** Lay out goals, objectives, and constraints; identify and involve stakeholders; estimate and allocate resources. (Tying ML to the business, covered above, lives here.)

## Framing ML Problems

Picture yourself as an ML tech lead at a bank for millennials. Your boss heard a rival sped up customer support 2× with ML and orders you to "use ML to speed up our support too." Slow support is a *problem* — but it is **not yet an ML problem**. An ML problem is defined by three things: **inputs**, **outputs**, and an **objective function** to guide learning. None of those are in your boss's request. Framing them is your job.

Digging in, you find the real bottleneck: requests must be routed to one of four departments — accounting, inventory, HR, IT. That's a **classification** problem. **Input:** the customer request. **Output:** the department. **Objective function:** minimize the gap between predicted and actual department. How you frame a request like this can make your job dramatically easier or harder.

### Types of ML Tasks

The model's **output** dictates the **task type**. The two most general families are **classification** (sort inputs into categories) and **regression** (output a continuous value). Classification splits further:

**Classification vs. regression — and reframing between them.** A spam filter (spam / not-spam) is classification; a house-price model is regression. The two are surprisingly interchangeable. **Regression → classification:** bucket house prices (under \$100k, \$100k–\$200k, \$200k–\$500k, …) and predict the bucket. **Classification → regression:** have the spam model emit a value in 0–1 and threshold it (above 0.5 ⇒ spam).

**Binary vs. multiclass.** Fewer classes is simpler. **Binary** (exactly two classes) is the simplest — toxic or not, cancerous or not, fraudulent or not — and metrics like F1 and confusion matrices are far more intuitive with two classes. With more than two it's **multiclass**. When the class count gets large — thousands of diseases, tens of thousands of products — the task has **high cardinality**, which is genuinely hard:

::: callout warning "High-cardinality classification is a data problem first"
A rough rule of thumb: a model needs **~100 examples per class** to learn it. With 1,000 classes that's already **100,000+ examples** — and the rare classes (there will be many) are exactly the ones hardest to collect. A common remedy is **hierarchical classification**: a first classifier sorts into a few broad groups (electronics / home & kitchen / fashion / pet supplies), then a second classifier resolves the subgroup (within fashion: shoes / shirts / jeans / accessories).
:::

**Multiclass vs. multilabel.** In binary and multiclass, each example belongs to *exactly one* class. When an example can belong to **several** classes at once — an article that is both *tech* and *finance* — it's **multilabel**. Two standard approaches:

1. **Multi-hot vector.** Among `[tech, entertainment, finance, politics]`, a *tech+finance* article is labeled `[1, 0, 1, 0]` (versus a single-label `[0, 1, 0, 0]`).
2. **Set of binaries.** Train four independent yes/no models, one per topic.

Multilabel is the task the author has seen companies struggle with most. The number of classes per example *varies*, which causes two pains. **Labeling** gets messy (one annotator says two classes, another says one — the **label multiplicity** problem). And **extracting predictions** from raw probabilities is awkward: given `[0.45, 0.2, 0.02, 0.33]`, a multiclass model just takes the top one (0.45), but a multilabel model has no fixed count — do you take the top two (0.45, 0.33)? The top three?

::: callout example "Reframing 'next app to open' — same problem, much better setup"
**Naive framing (multiclass):** input the user/context features, output a probability over *all N apps*. One prediction per user, a length-N vector. The flaw: adding a new app means retraining the whole model (its size depends on N). **Better framing (regression):** input the user, context, **and one app's features**, output a single 0–1 score for *that* app. Now there are N predictions per user, but each is just a number — and a **new app is just a new input**, no retraining required. A small change in framing erased a painful maintenance problem.
:::

### Objective Functions

To learn, a model needs an **objective function** to optimize — usually called a **loss function**, because we typically **minimize** the loss caused by wrong predictions. In supervised learning, loss compares the model's outputs against ground-truth labels using a measure like **RMSE** (root mean squared error) or **cross entropy**.

For example, an article whose true label is `[0, 0, 0, 1]` (politics) but whose model output is `[0.45, 0.2, 0.02, 0.33]` has a **cross-entropy loss** equal to the cross entropy of the prediction relative to the truth — a single number the optimizer pushes downward.

Choosing a loss function is *usually* straightforward — not because losses are simple, but because crafting a novel one needs real math, so most engineers reach for the standard ones:

| Task | Common loss function(s) |
|---|---|
| **Regression** | RMSE (root mean squared error) or MAE (mean absolute error) |
| **Binary classification** | Logistic loss (a.k.a. **log loss**) |
| **Multiclass classification** | Cross entropy |

#### Decoupling objectives

Framing gets genuinely tricky when you must minimize **multiple** objectives at once. Say you rank posts in a newsfeed to **maximize engagement**, via three objectives: filter spam, filter NSFW, and rank by predicted clicks. You soon learn that optimizing engagement alone rewards **extreme content** (it gets the most clicks). So you adopt a wiser goal — maximize engagement *while* curbing misinformation — adding objectives to **filter misinformation** and **rank by quality**. Now two objectives **conflict**: an engaging-but-low-quality post — rank it high or low?

Each objective is its own loss. You want to minimize `quality_loss` (predicted vs. true quality) *and* `engagement_loss` (predicted vs. true clicks). There are two ways to combine them:

- **One combined loss, one model.** Train a single model on `loss = α · quality_loss + β · engagement_loss` and tune α, β (formally, this is **Pareto optimization**). The pain: every time you re-tune α and β, you must **retrain** the model.
- **Decouple — one model per objective, then combine.** Train a `quality_model` (minimizes `quality_loss`) and an `engagement_model` (minimizes `engagement_loss`) **separately**, then rank by the combined score `α · quality_score + β · engagement_score`. Now you can re-tune α and β **without retraining anything.**

## Mind Versus Data

A decade of progress suggests an ML system's success rides largely on its **data** — so most companies pour effort into managing and improving data rather than inventing better algorithms. But plenty of researchers are skeptical that data is the whole story. At nearly every conference, a debate flares up: **mind vs. data** (where "mind" hides in *inductive biases* and *clever architectures*, and "data" travels with *computation*, since more data usually means more compute). In principle you can pursue both — in practice, time on one is time off the other.

**The mind camp.** **Judea Pearl** (Turing Award, causal inference) titles a chapter "Mind over Data" and insists *"data is profoundly dumb,"* warning that data-centric ML folks could be *"outdated, if not jobless"* in 3–5 years. More mildly, **Christopher Manning** (Stanford AI Lab) argues that huge compute plus huge data plus a *simple* learner makes a *bad* learner — **structure** lets us learn more from less data.

**The data camp.** **Richard Sutton's** "Bitter Lesson": across 70 years of AI, *"general methods that leverage computation are ultimately the most effective, and by a large margin,"* while human-knowledge shortcuts help short-term but lose long-term. Asked why Google Search worked so well, **Peter Norvig** replied: *"We don't have better algorithms. We just have more data."* And **Monica Rogati's** "AI hierarchy of needs" places data at the foundation — without data, there is no data science.

::: callout key "The pragmatic verdict — for now"
The real question isn't whether *finite* data is **necessary** (everyone agrees it is) but whether it's **sufficient** — and "finite" matters, because with *infinite* data you could just look up the answer. Whichever camp ultimately wins, **data is indispensable today.** Models keep getting bigger and hungrier: the 2013 One Billion Word Benchmark (0.8B tokens) gave way to GPT-2's ~10B and GPT-3's ~500B tokens. But more data isn't a free lunch — **more low-quality data** (stale or mislabeled) can actively *hurt* performance. Quantity *and* quality.
:::

## Summary

Every project must start with **why**, and ML is no exception. If a system is built for a business, it must be motivated by **business objectives** translated into **ML objectives** — because companies don't care about ML metrics unless they move business metrics. Before building, you pin down four **requirements** (reliability, scalability, maintainability, adaptability), then follow an **iterative, never-ending process** that loops through scoping, data, modeling, deployment, monitoring, and business analysis. Along the way you must **frame** vague requests as concrete ML tasks (classification vs. regression, and their subtypes) with well-chosen **objective functions** — **decoupling** multiple objectives when they collide. And underneath it all sits the **mind-vs-data** debate, whose practical takeaway is clear: for now, the quality and quantity of your **data** matter enormously. The next chapters zoom into these building blocks, starting with the fundamentals of **data engineering**.

## Key takeaways

::: takeaways
- **Business metrics decide a project's fate.** Companies don't care about 94%→94.2% unless it moves revenue, churn, or another KPI — always wire your ML metric to a business metric, and use experiments (A/B tests) to prove the link.
- A good ML system satisfies four requirements: **reliability** (correct even under faults; beware *silent* failures), **scalability** (complexity, traffic, model count — via resource *and* artifact management), **maintainability** (diverse contributors collaborating with versioned, reproducible code/data/artifacts), and **adaptability** (update on new data/needs without downtime).
- Building ML is a **loop**, not a line: scoping → data engineering → model development → deployment → monitoring & continual learning → business analysis → back to scoping. You constantly revisit earlier steps.
- **Framing** turns a business request into an ML task defined by **inputs, outputs, and an objective function.** Pick the right task type — classification (binary / multiclass / multilabel) or regression — and remember the two can be reframed into each other.
- **High-cardinality** classification is a data problem first (~100 examples per class); **hierarchical classification** helps. **Multilabel** is the trickiest task (label multiplicity; ambiguous prediction extraction).
- Use standard **loss functions** (RMSE/MAE, log loss, cross entropy), and when you have **multiple objectives, decouple them** — train one model per objective and combine scores so you can re-tune weights without retraining.
- In **mind vs. data**, the question is whether finite data is *sufficient*, not *necessary*. For now, **data — in both quality and quantity — is indispensable**, but more low-quality data can hurt.
:::
