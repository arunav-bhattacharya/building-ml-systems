Chapters 4 through 6 built the *logic* of a model — turning raw data into training data, extracting features, choosing an architecture, and crafting metrics to evaluate it. That work usually belongs to the data‑science team and demands ML knowledge plus subject‑matter expertise. This chapter is about the next step in the loop: **deployment** — getting that model out of the notebook and into the hands of real users.

"Deploy" is a loose word. It just means making your model **running and accessible** — leaving the development environment for a staging or production one. And there's a brutally honest version of how hard that is: *deploying is easy if you ignore all the hard parts.* Wrapping a `predict` function in a Flask or FastAPI endpoint, sticking it in a container, and pushing it to AWS or GCP can take an hour. The hard parts are everything after: serving **millions of users at millisecond latency with 99% uptime**, getting paged the instant something breaks, figuring out *what* broke, and shipping the fix without downtime.

Production is a spectrum. For one team it means a tidy plot in a notebook for the business folks; for another it means keeping models alive for millions of people a day. This chapter — possibly the most *engineering*-heavy in the book — is for the second kind of team. The good news, as Chip Huyen stresses, is that deploying models is an **engineering challenge, not an ML challenge**.

::: callout note "Exporting a model = serialization"
To hand a model to another application, you **export** (or *serialize*) it — saving both its **definition** (architecture: layers, units) and its **parameter values** (the learned weights), usually together. In TensorFlow 2 you might call `tf.keras.Model.save()` to produce a *SavedModel*; in PyTorch you might use `torch.onnx.export()` to produce an **ONNX** file. Some companies have the model's authors deploy it; others hand it to a separate team — but that handoff adds communication overhead, slows updates, and makes debugging harder.
:::

## Machine Learning Deployment Myths

Because deploying ML differs from deploying ordinary software (Chapter 1), people who have never done it tend either to dread it or to badly underestimate it. Four myths come up again and again.

### Myth 1: You Only Deploy One or Two ML Models at a Time

Academic projects train a single model on a small problem, so newcomers picture production the same way — and build infrastructure that collapses the moment a second model shows up. Real companies run **many, many** models. One feature often needs its own model, and one app has many features. A ride‑sharing app like Uber needs separate models for ride demand, driver availability, ETA, dynamic pricing, fraud, churn, and more. Operate in 20 countries without models that generalize across cultures and languages, and each country needs its own set — 20 countries × 10 models = **200 models** before you blink.

The real numbers dwarf that. Uber runs **thousands** of models in production; at any instant Google has thousands training concurrently with hundreds of billions of parameters; Booking.com has **150+**. A 2021 Algorithmia study found that among organizations with over 25,000 employees, **41% run more than 100 models** in production.

::: callout key "Design for a fleet, not a model"
Your serving, monitoring, and CI/CD infrastructure should assume **hundreds** of models from day one. Infrastructure that only supports "the model" will not survive contact with a real product.
:::

### Myth 2: If We Don't Do Anything, Model Performance Remains the Same

Software doesn't age like fine wine — it ages poorly. A program that degrades over time even though "nothing changed" is suffering **software rot** (or *bit rot*), and ML is not immune. Worse, ML systems also face **data distribution shifts**: the data a model meets in production drifts away from the data it trained on (Chapter 8). The consequence is a rule of thumb worth tattooing on your wall: a model is usually **best the moment it finishes training and decays from there.**

### Myth 3: You Won't Need to Update Your Models as Much

People ask "how *often should* I update my models?" Huyen's answer: that's the wrong question. Ask **"how often *can* I update my models?"** Since performance decays, you want to refresh as fast as possible — borrowing the discipline DevOps figured out long ago. Even back in 2015, Etsy deployed **50×/day**, Netflix **thousands** of times a day, and AWS **every 11.7 seconds**. Many companies still retrain monthly or quarterly, but Weibo's iteration cycle for some models is **10 minutes**, with similar figures at Alibaba and ByteDance. As former Google/Slack engineer Josh Wills put it: *"We're always trying to bring new models into production just as fast as humanly possible."*

### Myth 4: Most ML Engineers Don't Need to Worry About Scale

"Scale" varies — maybe hundreds of queries per second, maybe millions of users a month. You might think only a handful of giants need to care: there's only one Google, one Facebook, one Amazon. True, but **a few large companies employ most of the engineering workforce.** The Stack Overflow 2019 survey found that over half of developers work at companies of **100+ employees**, and a company that size has a good chance of serving a meaningful number of users. A Twitter poll for ML roles came back the same. Statistically, then, **an ML engineer should care about scale.**

## Batch Prediction Versus Online Prediction

One foundational decision shapes both your users' experience and your developers' lives: **how does the system generate and serve its predictions — online or batch?** (Generating a prediction is called **inference**.) The vocabulary here is genuinely muddy, but if you forget everything else, remember these **three modes**:

- **Batch prediction** — uses only **batch features**.
- **Online prediction with only batch features** — e.g., precomputed embeddings.
- **Online prediction with batch *and* streaming features** — also called **streaming prediction**.

**Online prediction** generates and returns a prediction *as soon as a request arrives* — type an English sentence into Google Translate, get the French back immediately. It's also called **on‑demand prediction**, and when requests arrive over RESTful/HTTP APIs it's called **synchronous prediction** (predictions are produced in sync with requests).

**Batch prediction** generates predictions *periodically or on a trigger*, **stores** them (in SQL tables, an in‑memory store, etc.), and **looks them up** when needed. Netflix might recompute everyone's recommendations every four hours; when you log in, the precomputed list is simply fetched. It's also called **asynchronous prediction** (predictions are produced out of sync with requests).

::: callout note "Why the names are confusing"
Both modes can score many samples (a *batch*) or one at a time, so "batch" and "online" are imperfect. People reach for **synchronous** vs. **asynchronous** instead — but that's imperfect too: when online prediction uses a real‑time transport to deliver requests, the request and its prediction are technically asynchronous. Don't over‑index on the labels; focus on the behavior.
:::

The feature types decide what's possible. As Chapter 3 covered, features computed from historical data (databases, warehouses) are **batch features**; features computed from real‑time transports are **streaming features**. Batch prediction can only use batch features. Online prediction can use **both**. After you order on DoorDash, an ETA model might combine a **batch feature** — this restaurant's mean prep time historically — with **streaming features** — how many orders it has in the last 10 minutes and how many couriers are free right now.

::: callout note "Streaming features ≠ online features"
**Online features** are the broad category: *any* feature used for online prediction, including batch features pulled into memory. A classic example is **item embeddings** for session‑based recommendations — precomputed in batch, then fetched at request time. Those embeddings are online features but **not** streaming features. **Streaming features** mean *only* features computed from streaming data.
:::

The two modes aren't mutually exclusive. A common **hybrid**: precompute predictions for popular queries and serve the long tail online. In fact, DoorDash and UberEats use **batch** prediction for restaurant recommendations (too many restaurants to score live) but switch to **online** prediction for food‑item recommendations once you click into a restaurant. A common worry is that online prediction is less efficient — you can't always batch inputs to exploit vectorization — but that isn't necessarily true (Chapter 3's *batch vs. stream processing*). And online prediction **avoids wasted compute**: if only 2% of users log in daily (Grubhub had 31M users but 622K daily orders in 2020), precomputing for everyone throws away 98% of the work.

| | Batch prediction (asynchronous) | Online prediction (synchronous) |
|---|---|---|
| **Frequency** | Periodic — e.g., every four hours | As soon as a request arrives |
| **Useful for** | Processing accumulated data when you don't need results immediately (recommender systems) | When a prediction is needed the instant a sample appears (fraud detection) |
| **Optimized for** | High **throughput** | Low **latency** |
| **Feature types** | Batch features only | Batch and/or streaming features |
| **Responsiveness** | Stale until the next batch run | Reacts to the user's latest behavior |

### From Batch Prediction to Online Prediction

To anyone trained academically, **online** feels like the natural way to serve: feed an input, get a prediction. It's also the easiest first deployment — export the model, upload it to SageMaker or Google App Engine, and you get an **endpoint** (the URL where the prediction service lives); POST an input, get a prediction back.

The snag is **latency**: the model may simply take too long. Batch prediction is the workaround — precompute everything in advance, store it, and look it up later. Because predictions are already made, you stop worrying about model speed entirely; **a lookup is faster than a fresh computation**, so batch prediction doubles as a trick to hide the latency of heavy models. It's ideal when you want *lots* of predictions and don't need them right away — score every customer's likelihood to buy, then reach out to the top 10% (you needn't use them all).

But batch prediction has real downsides:

- **It's stale.** It can't react to changing preferences. Binge horror lately and Netflix floods you with horror; search "comedy" and start browsing, and Netflix *should* adapt — but it can't until the next batch runs. Mild annoyance here, but it ties directly to engagement and retention.
- **You must know the queries in advance.** Fine for "recommend movies for our known users" (new users get generic recommendations). Impossible for an English→French translator — you can't enumerate every possible sentence — so you *need* online prediction.

Netflix's staleness is a nuisance; elsewhere, batch prediction means **catastrophic failure**. Online prediction is non‑negotiable for high‑frequency trading, autonomous vehicles, voice assistants, face/fingerprint unlock, fall detection for elderly care, and fraud. Catching a fraudulent transaction three hours late beats never — but catching it **in real time** can stop it from going through at all.

::: callout key "Batch is a workaround, not the goal"
Why precompute a million predictions and wrestle with storing and retrieving them, if you could generate each one on demand at the **same cost and same speed**? Batch prediction exists only because online prediction isn't yet cheap or fast enough. As hardware gets faster and cheaper, **online prediction may become the default.**
:::

Companies are investing heavily to move from batch to online. Beating the latency challenge takes two ingredients:

1. A **(near) real‑time pipeline** — a streaming pipeline with a real‑time transport and a stream computation engine that ingests incoming data, extracts streaming features if needed, feeds the model, and returns a prediction in near real time.
2. A **model fast enough** for its users — milliseconds, for most consumer apps. (Speeding up inference is what **model compression** and **model optimization**, below, are for.)

### Unifying Batch Pipeline and Streaming Pipeline

Batch prediction is largely a legacy artifact. For a decade, big‑data processing was dominated by **batch** systems — MapReduce, Spark — that crunch huge volumes efficiently but periodically. When companies first added ML, they reused those batch systems. The moment they wanted streaming features for online prediction, they had to **bolt on a separate streaming pipeline** — and now they have two.

Consider a Google‑Maps‑style ETA model, continually updated as a trip progresses. A useful feature is the **average speed of all cars on your route in the last five minutes**. For *training* you pull last month's data into a dataframe and compute the feature across many samples at once — a **batch** process. During *inference* the same feature is computed on a **sliding window** — a **streaming** process. Same feature, two completely different code paths.

Two pipelines for one feature is a **notorious source of production bugs**: change one pipeline, forget to mirror it in the other, and the two now extract subtly different features. It's worst when different teams own them — the ML team maintains the batch (training) pipeline while the deployment team maintains the stream (inference) pipeline — so the very features the model trained on no longer match the ones it sees live.

::: callout warning "The two‑pipeline trap (training/serving skew)"
Maintaining one batch pipeline for training and a separate streaming pipeline for inference invites **training/serving skew**: a feature defined two ways drifts apart, and the model silently scores garbage. Unify the pipelines, or enforce consistency with a shared definition.
:::

The fix is **unifying stream and batch processing**, a hot infrastructure topic in recent years. Uber and Weibo did major overhauls to consolidate onto a **stream processor like Apache Flink**, so a single feature definition serves both training and inference. Others use **feature stores** to keep batch‑training features and streaming‑serving features consistent (Chapter 10).

## Model Compression

A near‑real‑time pipeline gets *data* to the model fast, but that's not enough — the **model itself** has to infer fast. If it's too slow, you have three levers: make inference **faster** (inference optimization), make the model **smaller** (model compression), or make the **hardware** faster. Making a model smaller is **model compression**; making it infer faster is **inference optimization**. Compression began as a way to fit models onto edge devices, but smaller models usually run faster too.

The field is exploding — by April 2022, *Awesome Open Source* listed the "Top 168 Model Compression" projects, and counting. Four techniques dominate.

### Low-Rank Factorization

The idea: replace **high‑dimensional tensors with lower‑dimensional ones**. A prominent flavor is **compact convolutional filters**, where over‑parameterized convolutions are swapped for compact blocks that cut parameters and boost speed.

- **SqueezeNet** reaches **AlexNet‑level accuracy on ImageNet with ~50× fewer parameters**, partly by replacing 3×3 convolutions with 1×1.
- **MobileNets** factor a standard $K \times K \times C$ convolution into a **depthwise** convolution ($K \times K \times 1$) followed by a **pointwise** one ($1 \times 1 \times C$). Each new convolution costs only $K^2 + C$ parameters instead of $K^2 C$ — at $K = 3$, an **8–9× reduction**.

The catch: these methods are **architecture‑specific** (compact convolutional filters only help convolutional nets) and demand deep architectural know‑how to design, so they aren't yet broadly applicable.

### Knowledge Distillation

Train a small model (the **student**) to **mimic** a larger model or ensemble (the **teacher**); you deploy the student. The student is usually trained after a pretrained teacher, though the two can be trained together. The poster child is **DistilBERT**, which shrinks BERT by **40%** while keeping **97%** of its language‑understanding ability and running **60% faster**.

A pretrained teacher means the student needs less data and trains faster; no teacher means you must train one first, which costs far more data and time. It's also sensitive to the application and architectures, so it hasn't found wide production use.

### Pruning

**Pruning** originated with decision trees — cut branches that are uncritical or redundant. For neural networks (which tend to be over‑parameterized) it has two meanings:

1. **Remove entire nodes**, changing the architecture and cutting the parameter count.
2. **(more common)** Find the parameters least useful to predictions and **set them to 0**. The total parameter count is unchanged — only the number of *nonzero* parameters drops. This still shrinks the model, because a **sparse** network needs less storage than a dense one.

Experiments show pruning can cut **nonzero parameters by over 90%**, shrinking storage and speeding inference without hurting overall accuracy. (Chapter 11 notes pruning can introduce **bias**.)

::: callout note "Does pruning actually help — and how?"
Pruning works, but *why* is debated. **Liu et al.** argue the value isn't in the inherited "important weights" but in the **pruned architecture itself** — sometimes pruning is best viewed as architecture search, with the result **retrained from scratch** as a dense model. **Zhu et al.**, however, found the **large sparse** pruned model beat its retrained dense counterpart. (See also the *Lottery Ticket Hypothesis*.)
:::

### Quantization

**Quantization** is the most general and most widely used method — easy to do, and it generalizes across tasks and architectures. It shrinks a model by using **fewer bits per parameter.**

By default, most frameworks store a float in **32 bits** (single precision). A 100M‑parameter model then needs **400 MB**. Drop to **16 bits** (*half precision*) and you halve the footprint. Go all the way to **8‑bit integers** ("fixed point") for a quarter. In the extreme, **1‑bit** weights (binary‑weight networks like **BinaryConnect** and **XNOR‑Net**) push it further — the XNOR‑Net authors spun out Xnor.ai, which **Apple acquired for a reported \$200M** in early 2020.

Quantization helps **speed**, not just memory. It lets you raise batch size, and lower precision is simply faster to compute: adding two numbers bit‑by‑bit at $x$ ns/bit costs $32x$ ns for 32‑bit numbers but only $16x$ ns for 16‑bit ones. That cuts both training time and inference latency.

::: callout warning "The cost of fewer bits"
Fewer bits means a **smaller representable range**. Values outside it must be **rounded** and/or **scaled**, introducing **rounding errors** — and small rounding errors can cause big performance swings, or even **underflow/overflow to 0**. Efficient low‑level rounding and scaling is tricky, but the major frameworks handle it for you.
:::

Quantization happens either **during training** (*quantization‑aware training*, which lets you fit larger models on the same hardware) or **post‑training** (train in single precision, then quantize for inference). Low‑precision training is now well supported: **NVIDIA Tensor Cores** do mixed precision, and **Google TPUs** train in **Bfloat16** — "the secret to high performance on Cloud TPUs." **Fixed‑point inference is now an industry standard**; some edge devices support *only* fixed point, and on‑device runtimes like **TensorFlow Lite**, **PyTorch Mobile**, and **NVIDIA TensorRT** offer post‑training quantization in a few lines of code.

::: callout example "Case study — Roblox serves 1B+ BERT requests/day on CPUs"
Roblox needed **25,000+ inferences/second at under 20 ms** for NLP services — on **CPUs**. They started with a large BERT with fixed‑shape input, swapped in **DistilBERT** with dynamic‑shape input, then **quantized**. The biggest win came from quantization: 32‑bit float → 8‑bit int cut **latency ~7×** and raised **throughput ~8×**. (Take it with a grain of salt — the writeup doesn't report output‑quality changes after each step.)
:::

## ML on the Cloud and on the Edge

Where should the computation run — on the **cloud** or on the **edge**? *Cloud* means most compute happens on public or private cloud servers. *Edge* means most compute happens on consumer **edge devices**: browsers, phones, laptops, smartwatches, cars, security cameras, robots, embedded boards, **FPGAs**, and **ASICs**.

The easiest path is a managed cloud service (AWS, GCP), and that's how most companies start — clouds have made productionizing ML genuinely easy. But cloud has serious downsides, and edge has real pull.

The **cloud's** central problems are **cost** and **network dependence**. ML is compute‑hungry and compute is pricey — even in 2018, Pinterest, Infor, and Intuit each spent **hundreds of millions a year** on cloud, while small/medium companies spend **\$50K–\$2M**. Mishandle it and a startup can go bankrupt.

The **edge** is appealing for several reasons:

- **It runs where the cloud can't.** Cloud models need a stable connection to ship data back and forth; edge models work with **no internet or flaky internet** — rural areas, developing regions, or clients with strict **no‑internet policies**.
- **No network latency.** Often the network — not inference — is the bottleneck. You might shave ResNet‑50 inference from 30 ms to 20 ms, but **network latency can run to seconds**. On‑device, that disappears.
- **Privacy.** Cloud ML sends user data over networks (interceptable) and pools many users' data in one place (one breach hits everyone) — *"nearly 80% of companies experienced a cloud data breach in the past 18 months."* Keeping data on the device eases **GDPR** compliance. It doesn't eliminate privacy risk, though: an attacker can just **walk off with the device.**

The trade‑off is that edge devices must be **powerful enough**, have **enough memory** to hold and load the model, and have **enough battery** (running a full‑size BERT on a phone is a fast way to kill it). Because the upside is so large, **Google, Apple, and Tesla are all building their own chips**, ML‑hardware startups have raised **billions**, and by 2025 there should be **30+ billion active edge devices** worldwide. Which raises the question: how do you make a model run efficiently on *arbitrary* hardware?

### Compiling and Optimizing Models for Edge Devices

For a model built in, say, TensorFlow or PyTorch to run on a given hardware backend, that **framework has to be supported by that hardware**. The gap is real: TPUs launched publicly in February 2018, but PyTorch wasn't supported on them until **September 2020**.

Providing that support is brutally labor‑intensive, because **every backend has different memory layouts and compute primitives.** A CPU's primitive used to be a **scalar** (a number), a GPU's a **1‑D vector**, and a TPU's a **2‑D vector** (tensor) — so a convolution looks completely different on each, and you must juggle distinct L1/L2/L3 cache layouts and buffer sizes. The result is a combinatorial mess: framework developers support only a few server‑class chips, hardware vendors ship kernel libraries for only a few frameworks, and deploying to anything new takes huge manual effort.

The escape is a **middleman** between frameworks and hardware. Frameworks translate their code into the middleman; hardware vendors support the middleman once instead of many frameworks. That middleman is an **intermediate representation (IR)**, and IRs sit at the heart of how **compilers** work. From your model's original code, a compiler emits a **series of high‑ and low‑level IRs** before producing hardware‑native machine code.

This sequence is called **lowering** — you "lower" high‑level framework code toward low‑level hardware‑native code. It's *not* translation, because there's **no one‑to‑one mapping**. **High‑level IRs** are usually **computation graphs**: a graph describing the order of operations in your model.

::: callout analogy "An IR is like a universal travel adapter"
Without it, every appliance (framework) needs a custom plug for every country's socket (hardware) — an impossible matrix. A **universal adapter** (the IR) sits in the middle: appliances plug into it, and it plugs into each socket. Now adding one new appliance or one new country means supporting **one** interface, not all of them.
:::

**Model optimization.** Lowered code *runs*, but maybe not *fast* — it may ignore data locality and caches, or skip vector/parallel features. The problem compounds across a real ML stack: pandas/dask/ray for features, NumPy for vectorization, Hugging Face Transformers for embeddings, then an ensemble of sklearn / TensorFlow / LightGBM. Each function may be optimized internally, but there's **little optimization *across* frameworks** — and naively shuffling data between them can cause an order‑of‑magnitude slowdown. Stanford's **DAWN lab** found typical NumPy/pandas/TensorFlow workloads run **23× slower** single‑threaded than hand‑optimized code.

In practice, models that look fine in development turn out too slow once deployed, so companies hire **optimization engineers** — experts in *both* ML and hardware, hard to find and expensive. The alternative is an **optimizing compiler** that automates it. Optimization comes in two scopes:

- **Local** — optimize one operator or a small set. Standard tricks: **vectorization** (process contiguous elements together to cut I/O latency), **parallelization** (split an array into independent chunks), **loop tiling** (reorder loop accesses to suit the hardware's cache — and a good CPU pattern is a bad GPU pattern), and **operator fusion** (merge operators so two operations on an array share one loop instead of two).
- **Global** — optimize the **whole computation graph** end to end, e.g., fusing a convolutional net **vertically or horizontally** to slash memory access.

**Using ML to optimize ML.** Given operators A, B, C, you could fuse A+B, B+C, or all three — a combinatorial space of execution plans. Traditionally, vendor optimization engineers hand‑craft **heuristics** (an NVIDIA team making ResNet‑50 fly on a DGX A100). Two drawbacks: heuristics are **nonoptimal** (no guarantee they're best) and **nonadaptive** (each new framework or chip means redoing the work — and optimizing a CNN differs from an RNN differs from a transformer). Vendors optimize popular models like ResNet‑50 and BERT; invent a *new* architecture and you may have to optimize it yourself before anyone adopts it.

Brute‑forcing every plan is intractable, but approximating intractable problems is exactly **what ML is good at** — so use ML to **prune the search space** and **predict** each plan's runtime. PyTorch's `torch.backends.cudnn.benchmark=True` enables **cuDNN autotune**, which searches preset options for the fastest convolution — but only for convolutions. A far more general tool is **autoTVM**, part of the open‑source **TVM** compiler stack, which works on **subgraphs**:

1. Break the computation graph into **subgraphs**.
2. **Predict** each subgraph's size.
3. Allocate time to **search** each subgraph for its best plan.
4. **Stitch** the best per‑subgraph plans together to run the whole graph.

autoTVM measures **actual runtimes** as ground truth to train a cost model predicting future paths — so it **adapts to whatever hardware it runs on**. The downside is the cost model is slow to warm up: searching all paths can take **hours or days**. But it's a **one‑time** cost — results are **cached**, reused, and seed future tuning — so you optimize once per hardware type, then run on every device of that type. For ResNet‑50 on an NVIDIA TITAN X, autoTVM takes ~70 trials to beat cuDNN.

::: callout warning "Don't over‑read benchmarks"
A popular model running blazing‑fast on some hardware doesn't mean an *arbitrary* model will — it may simply be **over‑optimized** for that benchmark. Treat MLPerf‑style leaderboards with caution.
:::

### ML in Browsers

Compilers target specific hardware — but there's a way to hit **any** device that has a browser (MacBooks, Chromebooks, iPhones, Android). Run the model in the **browser** and you stop caring what chip is underneath; if Apple swaps Intel for ARM, it's not your problem.

Browsers suggest **JavaScript**, and tools like **TensorFlow.js**, Synaptic, and brain.js can compile models to it — but JavaScript is **slow** and awkward for heavy logic like feature extraction. The stronger option is **WebAssembly (WASM)**, an open standard for running executables in browsers. Build in scikit‑learn / PyTorch / TensorFlow, compile to **WASM** instead of to specific hardware, and you get an executable you call from JavaScript. WASM is performant, easy to use, and growing fast — **supported by ~93% of devices** as of September 2021. Its drawback: running in a browser, it's still **slower than native** — about **45% slower on Firefox to 55% slower on Chrome** in one study.

## Key takeaways

::: takeaways
- "Deploy" just means **running and accessible**; the easy demo takes an hour, but the hard parts — millisecond latency, 99% uptime, alerting, debugging, seamless updates — are an **engineering** challenge, not an ML one.
- Bust the myths: real companies run **hundreds** of models; models **decay** from the moment they're trained (software rot + data shift); update **as fast as you can**; and **most ML engineers do need to worry about scale.**
- Three prediction modes: **batch** (batch features only), **online with batch features**, and **online with streaming features** (= *streaming prediction*). Batch optimizes **throughput**; online optimizes **latency**.
- **Batch prediction is a workaround** for slow/expensive online prediction — it precomputes and looks up, but it's **stale** and needs queries known in advance. As hardware improves, online prediction may become the default.
- One batch pipeline + one streaming pipeline = **training/serving skew** and bugs. Unify them with a **stream processor (Apache Flink)** or **feature stores.**
- Shrink and speed up models with **low‑rank factorization**, **knowledge distillation** (DistilBERT), **pruning** (zero/remove weights), and **quantization** (fewer bits — usually the biggest, easiest win).
- **Edge** beats cloud on cost, network‑independence, latency, and privacy — if the device has the compute, memory, and battery. **ML compilers** *lower* models through **IRs** to hardware‑native code, **autoTVM** uses ML to optimize ML, and **WASM** runs models in any browser.
:::
