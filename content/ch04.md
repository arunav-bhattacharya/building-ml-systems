ML curricula obsess over models — the "fun" part. But ask anyone who has shipped ML to production and they'll tell you the truth: you spend far more time wrestling data than tuning networks. Building a state-of-the-art model is exciting; spending a week untangling a malformed dataset that won't even fit in memory is not. Yet **that data work is what decides whether your system lives or dies.** No algorithm, however clever, can outrun bad training data.

This chapter is about getting *good* training data. "Training data" here means everything you feed the model during development — the **train, validation, and test splits** alike. We deliberately say *training data*, not *training dataset*, because a "dataset" sounds finite and frozen, while real production data is neither: it keeps arriving and keeps changing. We'll walk through how to **sample** the right data, how to **label** it (and what to do when labels are scarce), how to cope with **class imbalance**, and how to manufacture more data through **augmentation**.

One caution to keep front of mind throughout: data is full of hidden biases — introduced while collecting, sampling, or labeling, or baked into historical records that encode human prejudice. A model trained on biased data will faithfully reproduce that bias. **Use your data, but don't trust it blindly.**

## Sampling

Sampling is woven through the entire ML lifecycle, yet it rarely gets a mention in coursework. You sample real-world data to build a training set; you sample that set into train/validation/test splits; you even sample live events for monitoring. Sometimes sampling is unavoidable — you can't access *all* the data in the world, or you can't afford to process every row you do have. Often it's simply smart: a quick experiment on a small slice tells you whether a new model is promising before you commit to training on everything.

Understanding sampling methods pays off twice: it helps you **avoid sampling bias**, and it helps you sample *efficiently* — getting more signal from less data. There are two broad families: **nonprobability sampling** (no randomness) and **random (probability-based) sampling**.

### Nonprobability Sampling

In **nonprobability sampling**, data isn't chosen by any random rule — it's chosen by convenience or judgment. The common variants:

- **Convenience sampling** — take whatever data is easiest to get. Popular precisely because it's convenient.
- **Snowball sampling** — grow the sample from existing samples. To scrape legitimate Twitter accounts without database access, start with a handful, scrape everyone they follow, then repeat.
- **Judgment sampling** — experts decide what to include.
- **Quota sampling** — fill fixed quotas per slice with no randomization, e.g. exactly 100 survey responses from each age bracket regardless of the true age distribution.

The catch is that these samples are **not representative** of the real world, so they're riddled with **selection bias**. You might conclude this family is a bad way to pick ML training data — and you'd be right. Yet in practice, convenience still drives a startling amount of real ML:

::: callout warning "Convenience bias hides in famous datasets"
- **Language models** are trained not on representative text but on what's easy to scrape — Wikipedia, Common Crawl, Reddit.
- **Sentiment analysis** data often comes from IMDb and Amazon reviews (they ship natural rating labels), which over-represent people willing to post reviews online.
- **Self-driving car** data came largely from Phoenix (lax regulations) and the Bay Area (where the companies are) — both sunny. Waymo expanded to rainy Kirkland, WA in 2016, but sunny-weather data still dominates rainy or snowy.
:::

Nonprobability sampling is a fine way to bootstrap a project quickly. For reliable models, move to probability-based methods.

### Simple Random Sampling

The simplest probability method: give every sample an **equal chance** of selection. Want 10% of the population? Each member gets a 10% draw. It's trivial to implement — but **rare categories can vanish.** If a class is only 0.01% of the data and you sample 1%, you'll likely draw none of it, and a model trained on that sample may conclude the rare class doesn't exist.

### Stratified Sampling

To stop rare classes from disappearing, first split the population into the groups you care about — each group is a **stratum** — then sample from each separately. To take 1% of a two-class population, sample 1% of class A *and* 1% of class B; now both appear no matter how rare. The limitation: it isn't always possible to cleanly partition samples into groups — especially in **multilabel** tasks, where a single sample can belong to both class A *and* class B at once.

### Weighted Sampling

In **weighted sampling**, each sample gets a weight that sets its selection probability. Give samples A, B, C the weights 0.5, 0.3, 0.2 and they're drawn 50% / 30% / 20% of the time. This lets you bake in **domain knowledge** — weight recent data higher if you believe it's more valuable — and **correct distribution mismatch**: if your data is 25% red / 75% blue but the real world is 50/50, weight red samples 3× to rebalance them. In Python this is `random.choices(population=..., weights=..., k=...)`.

::: callout warning "Weighted sampling ≠ sample weights"
These sound alike but do different jobs. **Weighted sampling** decides *which* samples enter your training set. **Sample weights** (a.k.a. per-sample training weights) are assigned to samples *already in* the set and tell the **loss function** how much each one matters — higher-weight samples push the loss more and can noticeably shift the model's **decision boundary**.
:::

### Reservoir Sampling

**Reservoir sampling** is an elegant algorithm for sampling from a **stream** — exactly what you face in production. Picture a never-ending feed of tweets, and you want a fair sample of *k* of them. You don't know the total count *n* (so you can't compute a fixed per-item probability up front), and you can't hold the whole stream in memory. You need two guarantees: every tweet has an **equal** chance of being chosen, and you can **stop at any moment** with a correctly-sampled set in hand.

The algorithm keeps a fixed-size array — the **reservoir** of size *k* — and runs three steps:

1. **Fill it.** Put the first *k* elements straight into the reservoir.
2. **For each new *n*-th element**, draw a random integer *i* with $1 \le i \le n$.
3. **Maybe swap.** If $i \le k$, replace the *i*-th reservoir slot with the new element. Otherwise, discard it.

The magic: each incoming *n*-th element lands in the reservoir with probability $k/n$, and you can prove every element *already* in the reservoir also stays with probability $k/n$. So at any point, all samples seen so far have an equal chance of being in the reservoir — stop whenever you like and the sample is valid.

### Importance Sampling

**Importance sampling** lets you sample from a distribution $P(x)$ that's expensive or impossible to sample from, by sampling from an easier **proposal distribution** $Q(x)$ instead and reweighting each sample by $\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$. The only requirement is that $Q(x) > 0$ wherever $P(x) \ne 0$. In expectation the two are equal:

$$\mathbb{E}_{P(x)}[x] = \sum_x P(x)\,x = \sum_x Q(x)\,x\,\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} = \mathbb{E}_{Q(x)}\!\left[x\,\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\right]$$

A classic ML use is **policy-based reinforcement learning**. Evaluating a brand-new policy directly is costly, but if it's close to the old policy you can reuse rewards collected under the old one — the old policy's rewards form the proposal distribution — and reweight them toward the new policy.

| Method | How it picks samples | Best for / watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| **Convenience** | Whatever is easiest to obtain | Fast bootstrap; heavily biased |
| **Snowball** | Grow from existing samples | Hidden / hard-to-reach populations; bias |
| **Judgment** | Experts choose | Leverages expertise; subjective |
| **Quota** | Fixed counts per slice, no randomness | Guarantees coverage; not representative |
| **Simple random** | Equal probability for all | Easy; rare classes can vanish |
| **Stratified** | Sample each stratum separately | Guarantees rare classes; hard with multilabel |
| **Weighted** | Probability set by per-sample weight | Inject domain knowledge / fix mismatch |
| **Reservoir** | Stream into size-*k* array, swap with prob. *k/n* | Streaming, unknown-length data |
| **Importance** | Sample easy $Q$, reweight by $P/Q$ | Sampling from an intractable distribution |

## Labeling

Despite all the excitement around unsupervised learning, the vast majority of production ML is still **supervised** — it needs labeled data, and model quality tracks the **quality and quantity** of those labels. When Tesla's Andrej Karpathy decided to build an in-house labeling team, his recruiter asked how long he'd need it. His reply: "How long do we need an engineering team for?" Labeling has graduated from a chore into a **core, permanent function** of ML teams.

We'll look at three sources of labels, in the order they tend to occur to people: **hand labels**, **natural labels**, and — when both run dry — techniques for **handling the lack of labels**.

### Hand Labels

Anyone who has labeled production data knows it's painful, for several reasons:

- **Expensive** — especially when expertise is required. Twenty crowd-workers can be trained in 15 minutes to flag spam comments; labeling chest X-rays needs board-certified radiologists, whose time is scarce and costly.
- **Privacy risk** — someone has to *look* at your data. You can't ship confidential medical or financial records to a third party; data may not even be allowed to leave the building, forcing you to hire on-premises annotators.
- **Slow** — phonetic speech transcription can take **400×** the audio's length, so one hour of speech is ~400 hours (nearly 3 months) of work. The author's colleagues once waited almost a **year** for enough lung-cancer labels.

Slowness is the quiet killer: it throttles iteration and leaves your model unable to adapt. Suppose a sentiment model has two classes, NEGATIVE and POSITIVE, but PR decides angry tweets need fast handling and wants a third class, ANGRY. Now you must revisit your data, relabel examples as ANGRY, and possibly collect more — and the longer that takes, the further your live model drifts from what's needed.

#### Label multiplicity

To amass enough labels, teams pull from many sources and many annotators of varying skill — and those annotators **disagree**. This is the **label multiplicity** (or label ambiguity) problem: what do you do with conflicting labels for the same instance? Give three annotators this entity-recognition sentence —

> *Darth Sidious, known simply as the Emperor, was a Dark Lord of the Sith who reigned over the galaxy as Galactic Emperor of the First Galactic Empire.*

— and you may get back three *different* sets of entities (3, 6, and 4 entities respectively). A model trained on annotator 1's labels behaves very differently from one trained on annotator 2's. Disagreement worsens as required expertise rises: if one expert says A and another says B, what even is "ground truth," or "human-level performance"? The fixes are upstream: write a **clear problem definition** (e.g. "when entities nest, pick the longest substring"), then **bake that definition into annotator training** so everyone applies the same rules.

#### Data lineage

Indiscriminately blending data from many sources can make your model fail in baffling ways. Imagine a decent model trained on 100K samples; confident that more data helps, you pay to label another million. Performance *drops* — because the new million were crowdsourced to less-accurate annotators. Worse, if you've already mixed old and new, you can't tell them apart to diagnose it.

::: callout tip "Track data lineage"
Always record the **origin** of each sample and its labels — this is **data lineage**. It lets you flag biases and debug failures: if your model stumbles mostly on recently acquired data, lineage points you straight at how that batch was collected. More than once, the culprit turned out to be a spike of wrong labels in a fresh batch — not the model at all.
:::

### Natural Labels

If you're lucky, your task has **natural labels** — the system can evaluate the model's predictions automatically, no human annotation needed. Google Maps predicts your ETA; once you finish the trip it knows the true duration and can score the prediction. A stock-price model is graded two minutes later by the actual price. The canonical case is **recommender systems**: a recommended item that gets **clicked** is presumed good (POSITIVE); one ignored after, say, 10 minutes is presumed bad (NEGATIVE). Many problems can be reframed this way — predicting ad click-through is just recommending relevant ads. Labels inferred from behavior like clicks and ratings are called **behavioral labels**.

Even tasks without inherent natural labels can often be *engineered* to produce feedback: Google Translate lets users submit better translations; Facebook's Like button and reactions turn newsfeed ranking into a task with feedback. In the author's survey of 86 companies, **63% worked on tasks with natural labels** — probably because such tasks are cheaper to start with, not because 63% of ML problems naturally have them.

::: callout note "Implicit vs. explicit labels"
When a recommendation goes unclicked and you label it NEGATIVE, that's an **implicit label** — inferred from the *absence* of a positive signal. An **explicit label** is one the user deliberately gives: a low rating, a downvote, a thumbs-down. Implicit labels are abundant but noisier; explicit labels are scarcer but cleaner.
:::

#### Feedback loop length

For natural labels, the gap between serving a prediction and receiving its feedback is the **feedback loop length**. **Short loops** (minutes) are common in recommenders — clicking a related Amazon product or a Twitter follow suggestion happens fast. Longer content stretches it: blog posts or YouTube videos take hours; Stitch Fix can't know if you liked the clothes until you receive and try them, possibly **weeks** later.

Choosing the **window length** is a speed-vs-accuracy trade-off. A short window captures labels fast (great for catching model issues early) but risks **premature negatives** — marking a recommendation bad before a slow click arrives. No window fully eliminates this: a 2021 Twitter Ads study found most ad clicks come within five minutes, but some land *hours* later, so click counts tend to **underestimate** the true click-through rate.

::: callout example "Different feedback, different signal"
On an e-commerce app a user might click, add to cart, purchase, rate, review, or return. **Clicks** are frequent and fast (high volume) but weak signal; **purchases** are rarer but strongly tied to revenue. Optimize for clicks and you get lots of feedback to evaluate against; optimize for purchases and you get a signal closer to the business metric. Both are valid — which to pick deserves a real conversation among stakeholders.
:::

**Long feedback loops** (weeks to months) appear in **fraud detection**: a cardholder can dispute a charge for one to three months, and only after that dispute window closes with no complaint do you presume the transaction legitimate. Such labels are fine for quarterly reports but useless for catching problems early — a broken fraud model could let through enough fraud to bankrupt a small business before you ever notice.

#### Weak supervision

If hand labeling is so painful, why not skip it? **Weak supervision** generates labels programmatically from heuristics. The flagship open-source tool is **Snorkel**, built at the Stanford AI Lab. The insight: experts already use rules of thumb to label — *"if the nurse's note mentions pneumonia, treat the case as EMERGENT."* Snorkel encodes each rule as a **labeling function (LF)**:

```python
def labeling_function(note):
    if "pneumonia" in note:
        return "EMERGENT"
```

LFs can encode many heuristic types — **keywords**, **regular expressions**, **database lookups** (is the note's disease on a danger list?), and even **the outputs of other models**. Because heuristics are noisy, LF labels are noisy; multiple LFs may fire on one sample and **conflict**. So Snorkel **combines, denoises, and reweights** all LFs into the most-likely-correct labels. You technically need *zero* hand labels, but a small set helps you gauge LF accuracy and spot patterns for writing better LFs. This approach — generating labels with LFs — is called **programmatic labeling**.

| Hand labeling | Programmatic labeling |
|---|---|
| **Expensive**, especially with expertise | **Cost-saving** — expertise versioned, shared, reused |
| **No privacy** — data shipped to annotators | **Privacy** — write LFs on a cleared subsample, apply blindly to the rest |
| **Slow** — scales linearly with label count | **Fast** — scale 1K → 1M samples trivially |
| **Nonadaptive** — every change means relabeling | **Adaptive** — just reapply the LFs |

::: callout example "Eight hours vs. a year"
In a study with Stanford Medicine, models trained on weakly-supervised labels from a **single radiologist writing LFs for eight hours** matched models trained on labels from **nearly a year** of hand labeling. Two bonuses: the models kept improving with more *unlabeled* data even without new LFs, and six LFs were reused across the chest-X-ray and extremity-X-ray tasks.
:::

A natural question: if heuristics label so well, why train a model at all? Because LFs won't cover every sample — you train a model on the LF-labeled data so it can **generalize** to instances no LF touches. Weak supervision isn't flawless (labels can be too noisy to use), but it's a superb low-commitment way to test whether ML is worth pursuing before sinking money into hand labels.

#### Semi-supervision

Where weak supervision uses heuristics, **semi-supervision** uses **structural assumptions** to grow labels from a small **seed** set — and unlike weak supervision, it *requires* those initial labels. A few flavors:

- **Self-training** — train on your labeled seeds, predict on unlabeled data, add the **high-confidence** predictions to the training set, retrain, repeat until satisfied.
- **Similarity propagation** — assume samples sharing characteristics share labels. Label `#AI` as Computer Science; since `#ML` and `#BigData` appear in the same profile (MIT CSAIL), label them Computer Science too. When similarity isn't obvious, discover it with clustering or **k-nearest neighbors**.
- **Perturbation-based** — assume small perturbations don't change a label, so perturb training samples (add white noise to images, nudge word embeddings) to mint new labeled instances.

Done well, semi-supervision can rival fully-supervised performance even after most labels are discarded. With limited data, there's a tricky trade-off in how much to spend on a **validation set**: too small and the "best" model is just the one that overfit the validation set; too large and you'd have gained more by adding it to training. A common fix — pick the champion on a reasonably large validation set, then continue training that champion on the validation data too.

#### Transfer learning

**Transfer learning** reuses a model built for one task as the starting point for another. A **base model** is trained on a **base task** with cheap, abundant data — **language modeling** is ideal because it needs no labels (predict the next token from preceding ones). That base model is then applied to a **downstream task** like sentiment analysis or question answering. Sometimes — **zero-shot** — you use it directly; usually you **fine-tune**, continuing training on the downstream data. You may also **prompt** it with a template (a few Q/A pairs) to coax out the answer you want.

Transfer learning shines when labeled data is scarce, and even with plenty of data, starting from a pretrained model often beats training from scratch. It powers a huge share of production ML today — object detectors built on ImageNet-pretrained backbones, text classifiers on BERT or GPT-3 — and lowers the barrier to entry by slashing up-front labeling cost.

::: callout note "Bigger base models, but only a few can train them"
A clear recent trend: the **larger the pretrained base model, the better** it tends to do downstream. But large models are brutally expensive — GPT-3's training is estimated in the **tens of millions of dollars**. Many expect that only a handful of companies will train these giants, while everyone else uses them off-the-shelf or fine-tunes them.
:::

#### Active learning

**Active learning** (a.k.a. query learning) makes labeling *efficient*: rather than labeling random samples, the model itself **picks the samples most worth labeling**, hoping to hit higher accuracy with fewer labels. The simplest heuristic is **uncertainty** — label the examples the model is least confident about (e.g. the lowest predicted-class probability), since those most sharpen the decision boundary. On a toy two-Gaussian dataset, 30 *randomly* labeled samples gave 70% accuracy; 30 chosen by active learning gave **90%**.

Other heuristics include **query-by-committee** (an ensemble of models trained with different hyperparameters or data slices votes; label whatever they disagree on most) and picking samples that would yield the **largest gradient update** or **biggest loss reduction**. The samples can be **synthesized** by the model, drawn from a **stationary pool** of collected unlabeled data, or pulled from a **live stream** in production — the last is the most exciting, letting models adapt in real time as data shifts.

## Class Imbalance

**Class imbalance** is when the classes in your training data are wildly unequal in size. In a lung-cancer X-ray dataset, 99.99% of images may be normal lungs and only 0.01% cancerous. It strikes **regression** too: health-care bills are skewed, with a low median but an astronomical 95th percentile — and predicting that expensive tail accurately often matters far more than the median (a 100% error on a \$250 bill is tolerable; a 100% error on a \$10k bill is not).

### Challenges of Class Imbalance

Deep learning thrives on balanced data and struggles when classes are lopsided, for three reasons:

1. **Insufficient signal for the rare class.** With few minority instances, the problem degrades into **few-shot learning**; with *none*, the model may assume the rare class doesn't exist at all.
2. **Exploiting a trivial heuristic.** A model can get stuck always predicting the majority class. In the cancer example that scores **99.99% accuracy** while learning nothing useful — and that local optimum is hard for gradient descent to escape, since small tweaks only make accuracy worse.
3. **Asymmetric cost of errors.** Missing a cancerous X-ray is far costlier than mislabeling a normal one. If your loss treats every sample equally, you'll get a model that does fine on the majority but fails exactly where it matters most.

::: callout warning "The 99% accuracy trap"
With CANCER at 10% of the data, consider two models. **Model A** catches 10 of 100 cancers; **Model B** catches 90 of 100. You'd obviously want Model B in your corner — yet **both score 0.90 accuracy**, because the majority NORMAL class drowns out the signal. Overall accuracy is *useless* under imbalance. Score each class separately (A's accuracy on CANCER is 10%, B's is 90%) and reach for **precision, recall, F1**, the **ROC curve / AUC**, or — better still under heavy imbalance — the **Precision-Recall curve**, all of which focus on the positive (rare) class.
:::

Imbalance is the *norm* in the real world — rare events (fraud, churn, disease, dangerous defects) are usually the interesting ones. Classic examples: **fraud detection** (only ~6.8¢ per \$100 spent is fraudulent), **churn**, **disease screening**, **résumé screening** (98% filtered at first pass), and **object detection** (most candidate bounding boxes contain nothing). Imbalance can also be an *artifact* — e.g. spam is ~85% of all email, but most is filtered before reaching your database, so your sample looks the opposite — or simply a **labeling error**. Always investigate the cause first.

### Handling Class Imbalance

Beyond picking the **right metric** (above), two families of fixes exist: **data-level** (reshape the data) and **algorithm-level** (reshape the learning). Note that bigger, deeper networks tolerate imbalance better than shallow ones, and some argue you shouldn't "fix" imbalance that genuinely reflects reality — a good model should learn it. But building such a model is hard, so these techniques remain useful.

#### Data-level methods: resampling

**Resampling** rebalances the data. **Oversampling** adds minority instances; **undersampling** removes majority ones. The crude versions: randomly duplicate the minority, or randomly drop the majority.

- **Tomek links** (undersampling, 1976) — find pairs of opposite-class samples that sit close together and delete the majority member of each pair. This sharpens the boundary but can make the model **less robust**, since it never sees the messy true boundary.
- **SMOTE** (oversampling) — instead of copying, synthesize *new* minority samples as **convex (roughly linear) combinations** of existing minority points, interpolating between neighbors.

Both SMOTE and Tomek links are proven only on **low-dimensional** data; distance-based methods get expensive or infeasible in the high-dimensional spaces of large neural nets.

::: callout warning "Never evaluate on resampled data"
Resample only the *training* data. Evaluating on resampled data makes the model overfit that artificial distribution. Undersampling risks **discarding useful data**; oversampling risks **overfitting** on duplicated minority samples. Smarter schemes mitigate this: **two-phase learning** (train on undersampled data, then fine-tune on the original) and **dynamic sampling** (over-sample low-performing classes and under-sample high-performing ones *during* training — show the model less of what it knows and more of what it doesn't).
:::

#### Algorithm-level methods

Algorithm-level methods leave the data alone and **change the loss function**, since the loss steers learning. The ordinary loss averages every instance's loss equally:

$$L(X;\theta) = \sum_x \frac{1}{N} L(x;\theta)$$

The idea is to make costly mistakes hurt more so the model prioritizes them. Three approaches:

- **Cost-sensitive learning** (Elkan, 2001) — define a **cost matrix** $C_{ij}$ = cost of classifying class *i* as *j* (correct entries usually 0). If a false negative is twice as bad as a false positive, set $C_{10} = 2\,C_{01}$. The instance loss becomes $L(x;\theta) = \sum_j C_{ij}\,P(j \mid x;\theta)$. Downside: you must **hand-craft the matrix** for every task.
- **Class-balanced loss** — weight each class **inversely to its frequency** so rare classes count more: $W_i = \frac{N}{\text{# samples of class } i}$. A refined version weights by the **effective number of samples** to account for overlap among examples.
- **Focal loss** (Lin et al., 2017) — up-weight the **hard** examples. Adjust the loss so a sample with a *lower* probability of being correct gets a *higher* weight, pushing the model to focus on what it still gets wrong rather than coasting on easy cases.

**Ensembles** also empirically help with imbalance, though that's not usually why they're used (covered in Chapter 6).

### Simple Label-Preserving Transformations

The easiest technique: modify a sample while keeping its label. In **computer vision**, randomly **crop, flip, rotate, invert, or erase** parts of an image — a rotated dog is still a dog. PyTorch, TensorFlow, and Keras all support this; per the AlexNet paper, generating transforms on the CPU while the GPU trains the previous batch makes them "in effect, computationally free." In **NLP**, replace a word with a synonym — found via a thesaurus or via nearby **word embeddings** — assuming meaning and sentiment survive ("I'm so happy to see you" → "I'm so glad to see you"). A quick way to double or triple your data.

### Perturbation

**Perturbation** is also label-preserving, but it gets its own section because it's often used to *trick* models. Neural nets are surprisingly **noise-sensitive**: Su et al. showed that changing a **single pixel** flips the prediction on 67.97% of CIFAR-10 and 16.04% of ImageNet test images. Using deceptive inputs to fool a network is an **adversarial attack**, and the effect grows with image resolution.

The defense is to fight fire with fire: **add noisy / adversarial samples to training** so the model discovers and patches the weak spots in its decision boundary. Noise can be random or found by a search strategy — **DeepFool** computes the *minimum* perturbation needed to force a confident misclassification (this is **adversarial augmentation**). Adversarial augmentation is rarer in NLP (random pixels on a bear still look like a bear, but random characters in a sentence become gibberish), yet perturbation still helps: **BERT** randomly replaces a small fraction of tokens with random words during pretraining, and ablations show this gives a small boost.

### Data Synthesis

Since collecting data is slow, costly, and privacy-fraught, **synthesizing** some is appealing — even if synthesizing *all* of it remains out of reach.

- **Templates** (NLP) — cheaply bootstrap data by filling slots. From `"Find me a [CUISINE] restaurant within [NUMBER] miles of [LOCATION]"` plus lists of cuisines, sensible distances, and locations, you can mint thousands of chatbot training queries.
- **mixup** (vision) — blend two labeled examples into one with a continuous label. From a DOG ($x_1$, label 0) and a CAT ($x_2$, label 1), create $x' = \gamma x_1 + (1-\gamma)x_2$ with label $\gamma\cdot 0 + (1-\gamma)\cdot 1$. mixup improves generalization, reduces memorization of corrupt labels, hardens models against adversarial examples, and stabilizes GAN training.
- **GANs** — synthesize new examples with neural nets. Still mostly research, but Sandfort et al. boosted CT-segmentation performance significantly by adding **CycleGAN**-generated images to their data.

## Key takeaways

::: takeaways
- **Training data is the foundation** — no algorithm can compensate for bad data, so it's worth real effort to curate it. And because production data keeps shifting, building training data is an **iterative** process.
- There are two **sampling** families: **nonprobability** (convenience/snowball/judgment/quota) is fast but biased; **probability** (simple random, stratified, weighted, reservoir, importance) gives representative samples. Use **stratified** to protect rare classes and **reservoir** to fairly sample an unbounded stream with probability $k/n$.
- **Labels** come from **hand labeling** (expensive, slow, privacy-risky, prone to annotator disagreement — track **data lineage**) or **natural labels** (implicit clicks vs. explicit ratings, gated by **feedback-loop length**).
- When labels are scarce, lean on **weak supervision** (Snorkel labeling functions), **semi-supervision** (propagate from seeds), **transfer learning** (fine-tune a pretrained base model), or **active learning** (label only the most informative samples).
- **Class imbalance** starves the rare class of signal, invites trivial majority-class heuristics, and imposes asymmetric error costs — so **never trust accuracy**; use per-class metrics, F1, ROC/AUC, or the PR curve. Fix it at the **data level** (under/oversampling, SMOTE, Tomek links) or the **algorithm level** (cost-sensitive, class-balanced, or focal loss).
- **Data augmentation** multiplies data and hardens models via **label-preserving transforms**, **perturbation / adversarial samples**, and **synthesis** (templates, mixup, GANs).
:::

## Test your knowledge

```flashcards
Q: What's the core difference between nonprobability and probability sampling, and why does it matter?
A: Nonprobability sampling (convenience, snowball, judgment, quota) selects data with **no random rule**, so it carries **selection bias** and isn't representative. Probability sampling gives samples defined draw probabilities, yielding representative data. Use nonprobability only to bootstrap; use probability methods for reliable models.
---
Q: Walk through the three steps of reservoir sampling. What probability does each element end up with?
A: (1) Put the first *k* elements in the reservoir. (2) For the *n*-th incoming element, draw a random *i* in [1, n]. (3) If *i* ≤ *k*, replace reservoir slot *i* with the new element; else discard it. Every element ends up in the reservoir with probability **k/n**, so all samples are equally likely and you can stop anytime.
---
Q: Weighted sampling vs. sample weights — what's the difference?
A: **Weighted sampling** uses weights to decide *which* samples enter the training set. **Sample weights** are assigned to samples already in the set and tell the **loss function** how much each matters; higher-weight samples shift the decision boundary more.
---
Q: Why is overall accuracy a dangerous metric under class imbalance? What should you use instead?
A: The majority class dominates it — a cancer model that catches 10/100 cancers and one that catches 90/100 can both score 0.90. Use **per-class accuracy**, **precision/recall/F1**, **ROC/AUC**, or (best under heavy imbalance) the **Precision-Recall curve**, which focus on the rare positive class.
---
Q: Name the four techniques for handling a lack of labels and one line on each.
A: **Weak supervision** — heuristics/labeling functions generate noisy labels (Snorkel). **Semi-supervision** — propagate labels from a small seed set via structural assumptions. **Transfer learning** — reuse a pretrained base model, then fine-tune. **Active learning** — label only the most informative (e.g. most uncertain) samples.
---
Q: What is SMOTE and how does it differ from naïve oversampling?
A: SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) creates **new** minority samples as convex (roughly linear) **interpolations** between existing minority points, rather than just **duplicating** them — reducing the overfitting risk of plain oversampling. It's proven mainly on low-dimensional data.
---
Q: What does focal loss do, and what problem does it address?
A: Focal loss down-weights easy, well-classified examples and **up-weights hard ones** (lower predicted probability → higher weight), pushing the model to focus on what it still gets wrong. It's an algorithm-level fix for class imbalance.

Q: What is feedback-loop length, and why does choosing the window matter?
A: It's the time from serving a prediction to receiving its natural-label feedback. Short windows capture labels fast (good for catching issues early) but risk **premature negatives**; long windows (e.g. a 1–3 month fraud dispute window) give cleaner labels but are useless for fast issue detection.
```

```quiz
You're sampling tweets from an unbounded live stream you can't fit in memory, and you need every tweet to have an equal chance of selection. Which method fits?
- ( ) Stratified sampling
- ( ) Simple random sampling
- (x) Reservoir sampling
- ( ) Quota sampling
> Reservoir sampling is designed for streams of unknown length: it maintains a fixed-size reservoir and gives every element a k/n chance, so you can stop at any moment with a valid sample.

A model that always predicts "normal" on a dataset that is 99.99% normal lungs reports 99.99% accuracy. What does this illustrate?
- ( ) That the model has learned the cancer pattern well
- (x) The accuracy trap — under class imbalance, a trivial majority-class heuristic scores high while learning nothing useful
- ( ) That stratified sampling failed
- ( ) That focal loss was applied
> Under heavy imbalance, accuracy is dominated by the majority class. The model exploits a trivial heuristic, and gradient descent struggles to escape that local optimum — which is why per-class metrics are essential.

Which statement about weak supervision (e.g. Snorkel) is correct?
- ( ) It requires a large, fully hand-labeled dataset to begin
- (x) It encodes heuristics as labeling functions that programmatically generate (noisy) labels, needing no hand labels in principle
- ( ) It guarantees perfectly clean labels
- ( ) It only works for image data
> Weak supervision uses labeling functions (keywords, regexes, lookups, other models' outputs) to generate labels at scale. The labels are noisy and must be combined/denoised; a few hand labels help refine the LFs but aren't strictly required.

In active learning with an uncertainty heuristic, which samples does the model send to a human to label?
- ( ) Randomly chosen samples
- ( ) The samples it is most confident about
- (x) The samples it is least certain about
- ( ) Only samples from the majority class
> Active learning labels the most *informative* samples. The simplest heuristic is uncertainty — the examples the model is least sure about most help it refine the decision boundary, reaching higher accuracy with fewer labels.

What does mixup do?
- ( ) Removes majority-class samples that sit near the decision boundary
- ( ) Replaces a word with a synonym from a thesaurus
- (x) Blends two labeled examples into a new one with a continuous (interpolated) label
- ( ) Changes a single pixel to fool the model
> mixup synthesizes x' = γx₁ + (1−γ)x₂ with a correspondingly blended label. It improves generalization, reduces memorization of corrupt labels, and increases robustness to adversarial examples.
```
