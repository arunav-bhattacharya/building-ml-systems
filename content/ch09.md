In Chapter 8 we saw the many ways an ML system rots in production — above all, **data distribution shift**, where the world the model serves drifts away from the world it was trained on. We learned how to *detect* that drift. This chapter answers the obvious next question: once you've spotted the drift, **what do you do about it?**

The answer is to keep your models fresh by **updating them continually**. We'll start with what continual learning actually is (spoiler: it's far more an *infrastructure* problem than a fancy algorithm), lay out a four-stage plan to get there, and tackle the question every ML engineer eventually asks — *"how often should I retrain?"*

A retrained model can't be trusted on a stale, frozen test set alone. So we'll also cover a concept that sounds terrifying but is genuinely necessary: **test in production**. If monitoring (Chapter 8) is *passively* watching whatever model is live, test in production is *proactively* choosing which model gets to serve traffic so you can measure it safely. Both share one goal — understand how a model is really doing, and decide when to swap it.

::: callout note "Continual learning ≠ online learning ≠ continuous learning"
The author deliberately says **continual learning** to avoid two confusing neighbors. **"Online learning"** is often taken to mean updating on *every single incoming sample* — that's just one special, extreme case of continual learning. **"Continuous learning"** is even muddier: it sometimes means per-sample updates, sometimes the DevOps idea of *continuous delivery* of ML. In this book continual learning means updating in **batches or micro-batches**, as frequently as you choose.
:::

## Continual Learning

When people hear *"continual learning,"* they picture a model that retrains itself on every new example the instant it arrives. **Almost no company actually does that** — and for good reason:

- If the model is a neural network, learning one sample at a time invites **catastrophic forgetting**: the network abruptly and completely forgets what it learned before the moment it absorbs something new.
- It's wildly inefficient. Today's hardware is built for **batch** processing; feeding it one sample at a time squanders compute and can't exploit data parallelism.

So companies that *do* practice continual learning update in **micro-batches** — say, after every 512 or 1,024 examples. The right batch size is task-dependent.

Crucially, an updated model is **never shipped before it's evaluated**. You don't mutate the live model in place. Instead you **clone** it, train the clone on the new data, and only promote the clone if it proves better. The live model is the **champion**; the freshly-updated clone is the **challenger**. (Real systems often juggle several challengers at once and handle a losing challenger far more carefully than just deleting it — this is the simplified version.)

A second misconception is that continual learning means updating *constantly* — every 5 or 10 minutes. Most companies don't need that. They often lack the **traffic** to make such a tight schedule meaningful, and their models simply **don't decay** that fast. If moving from weekly to daily retraining buys you nothing and only adds overhead, don't do it. **Continual learning is about the *manner* of retraining, not a frantic frequency.**

The payoffs of stateful training are large:

- **Far less data.** Training from scratch may need every sample from the last three months; fine-tuning from yesterday's checkpoint needs only the last day's data.
- **Far less compute, faster convergence.** Grubhub reported that switching from daily *stateless* to daily *stateful* training cut their training compute cost **45×** and lifted purchase-through rate **20%**.
- **You may not need to store data at all.** In stateless retraining a sample is reused across many training runs, so it must be kept. In stateful training each sample is used **once**, so — in principle — you can train without permanently storing data. That's a quiet win for **privacy**, though most companies' keep-everything instincts mean it's rarely exploited.

::: callout key "Stateful doesn't mean *never* from scratch"
Teams that lean hardest on stateful training still **occasionally retrain from scratch** on a large dataset to *recalibrate* the model — or they run a from-scratch model in parallel and merge the two with techniques like a **parameter server**. Stateful training is a complement to from-scratch training, not a replacement.
:::

Once your infrastructure supports both, **frequency becomes a knob you turn** — hourly, daily, or "whenever a shift is detected." The deeper point: *continual learning is the infrastructure that lets you (a data scientist or ML engineer) update a model whenever needed — from scratch or by fine-tuning — and ship that update fast.*

#### Two kinds of model update

Stateful training raises a natural worry: *what if I want to add a new feature or a new layer?* That forces us to separate two update types — they behave very differently.

| | **Data iteration** | **Model iteration** |
|---|---|---|
| **What changes** | Same architecture, same features — refreshed on **new data** | A **new feature** is added, or the **architecture** itself changes |
| **Stateful training?** | Yes — this is where it's used today | Mostly **no** — usually requires training from scratch |
| **Research escape hatch** | — | Knowledge transfer (Google, 2015), **model surgery** (OpenAI, 2019) — promising, but no clear industry results yet |

As of the book's writing, **stateful training is mostly for data iteration**. Model iteration still generally means a from-scratch rebuild — though research on transferring trained weights between architectures hints this may loosen in the future.

### Why Continual Learning?

If continual learning is the ability to update and ship fast, *why* would you want that? Several reasons:

**1. Combat sudden distribution shifts.** Picture a ride-sharing pricing model (a.k.a. **dynamic pricing**). Thursday evenings in some neighborhood are historically quiet, so the model predicts low prices and few drivers bother showing up. But *this* Thursday there's a big event — demand spikes. If the model can't react fast by raising its price prediction and pulling drivers into the area, riders wait forever, hate the experience, and defect to a competitor.

**2. Adapt to rare and seasonal events.** Black Friday happens once a year — there's no way to amass enough historical data to nail customer behavior in advance, so the model must **learn through the day on fresh data**. Alibaba acquired Data Artisans (the team behind the stream processor **Apache Flink**) for \$103M partly to adapt Flink for ML, with a flagship use case of better recommendations during **Singles Day**, China's analogue to Black Friday.

**3. Overcome the continuous cold-start problem.** The classic **cold-start problem**: you must serve a brand-new user with *no history* (a recommender needs to know what you've watched, but you just arrived — so it falls back to generic "most popular" picks). The **continuous** cold-start problem generalizes this — it hits *existing* users too:

- A user switches from laptop to phone, and behaves differently on each.
- Users aren't logged in (most news sites don't require it).
- Users visit so rarely that their stored history is stale — Coveo found it common for an e-commerce site to have **over 70% of shoppers visit fewer than three times a year**.

If your model can't adapt within a user's *session*, it can't make relevant recommendations on a first or infrequent visit — and those users leave before the next scheduled update. **TikTok** is the cautionary marvel here: continual learning lets it tune its recommender to you **within minutes** of opening the app.

::: callout key "Reframe the question: why *not* continual learning?"
Continual learning is a **superset** of batch learning — it can do everything batch learning does, *plus* unlock cases batch learning can't. If it cost the same to set up and run, there'd be no reason not to use it. Today the setup cost is real (the next section explains why), but MLOps tooling is maturing fast — one day soon, standing up continual learning may be as easy as batch.
:::

### Continual Learning Challenges

Continual learning has many success stories, but three challenges stand between you and it: **fresh-data access**, **evaluation**, and **algorithms**.

Most companies pull training data from a **data warehouse** — but the warehouse is only as fresh as the data deposited into it, which can be slow, especially when many sources feed it. A faster path is to pull data **before** it lands in the warehouse, straight from **real-time transports** like **Kafka** or **Kinesis**.

Label computation can run as a slow **batch** job (wait for logs to hit the warehouse, then extract all labels at once) — but a much faster route is **stream processing**, extracting labels directly off the real-time transport. If labeling is still the bottleneck, you can speed it with **programmatic labeling** tools (e.g., **Snorkel**) or **crowdsourcing**. Streaming-first infrastructure is engineering-intensive today, but the tooling is exploding: **Confluent** (built on Kafka) hit a \$16B valuation, Snowflake launched a streaming team, and **Materialize** raised \$100M for a streaming SQL database.

#### Evaluation challenge

The hard part of continual learning **isn't writing the update** — that's a script. The hard part is making sure each update is **safe to deploy**. Continual learning *amplifies* the risk of catastrophic failures in two ways:

- **More updates = more chances to fail.** Every additional update is another roll of the dice.
- **More exposure to adversarial manipulation.** A model that learns online from real-world input is easier to poison with malicious data. In 2016, Microsoft's **Tay** chatbot learned from Twitter conversations — trolls flooded it with racist and misogynist messages, and within **16 hours** it was posting offensive tweets and had to be shut down.

So every update must be **thoroughly tested** before reaching a wide audience — offline evaluation (Chapter 6) *plus* online evaluation (this chapter). And remember: **evaluation itself takes time**, which can cap your update frequency. A major payment company the author worked with wanted to update its fraud-detection system fast to chase shifting fraud patterns — but because fraud is so rare, A/B testing needed about **two weeks** to accumulate enough fraudulent transactions to judge which model was better. So they could only update **every two weeks**.

#### Algorithm challenge

This is a *softer* challenge — it only bites **certain models** that want **fast (e.g., hourly)** updates. Specifically, **matrix-based and tree-based** models are awkward to update incrementally:

- A **neural network** can be updated with a batch of *any* size — even a single sample.
- A **collaborative-filtering** (matrix-based) model must first build the whole **user–item matrix** and run dimensionality reduction on it. You *can* redo that per update, but on a large matrix it's far too slow and expensive to run frequently.

Neural networks adapt to continual learning much more easily. Tree-based models *can* be made incremental — most notably the **Hoeffding Tree** and its variants (Hoeffding Window Tree, Hoeffding Adaptive Tree) — but these aren't yet widespread.

::: callout warning "Your feature code must go incremental too"
It's not just the learning algorithm. **Feature scaling** (Chapter 5) needs statistics like min/max/median/variance, which normally require a full pass over the data. If you recompute them per micro-batch, they **fluctuate wildly** between batches, so a model trained on one batch generalizes poorly to the next. The fix is **running statistics** — computing or approximating them incrementally as data streams in. Tools offer some support (sklearn's `StandardScaler.partial_fit`), but built-in methods are slow and cover a narrow range of statistics.
:::

**Stage 1 — Manual, stateless retraining.** Early on, the team races to ship *new* models for as many business problems as possible (fraud detection, recommendations, seller-abuse, shipping-time…). Maintaining old models takes a back seat: you update one only when its performance has degraded enough to do real harm *and* someone has spare time. Some models go six months, a quarter, or even a year without an update. The process is manual and ad hoc — one person queries the warehouse, another cleans and featurizes, retrains from scratch, exports a binary; someone else deploys it — and changes made during retraining often fail to reach production, breeding hard-to-trace bugs. **Most companies outside big tech** (those that adopted ML recently and lack an ML platform team) live here.

**Stage 2 — Automated retraining.** After a few years you have 5–10 models in production and your priority flips from *building* to *maintaining*. The ad-hoc pain grows unbearable, so you write a **script** that runs all retraining steps automatically on a schedule (e.g., via Spark). Most companies with reasonably mature ML infra are here. A few run experiments to find the optimal frequency; most set it by gut — *"nightly seems fine."* Watch out for two wrinkles: different models may need **different schedules** (product embeddings weekly, the ranking model daily), and **dependencies** between models (when embeddings change, the ranking model that consumes them must update too).

::: callout note "Stage 2 requirements — and the 'log and wait' trick"
The script must automatically: pull data → down/upsample → extract features → process labels → kick off training → evaluate → deploy. Three things make this feasible: a **scheduler** (Airflow, Argo); accessible **data**; and a **model store** to version artifacts (an S3 bucket at minimum; better, SageMaker or **MLflow**). A neat optimization is **"log and wait"**: the prediction service already extracted features for the live request, so reuse those exact features for retraining. It saves compute *and* kills **train–serving skew** by guaranteeing training features match serving features.
:::

**Stage 3 — Automated, stateful training.** Stateless retraining is costly at high frequency, so you reconfigure the script to **load the previous checkpoint** and continue training from it — *why retrain on three months of data daily when you can fine-tune on one day's?* The biggest requirement is a **mindset shift**: from-scratch retraining is so ingrained that many teams never even set up for stateful training. Once committed, the script change is easy — but you'll need **data and model lineage tracking** (which checkpoint a model descends from, and which data updated it) so you can reproduce and debug a tangle of versions like v3.32, v2.11, v1.64. No existing model store offers lineage tracking, so you'll likely **build it in-house**.

**Stage 4 — Continual learning.** Even in Stage 3, updates run on a **fixed schedule** — but the right schedule is situation-dependent (a quiet week barely decays your model; an eventful week decays it fast). In Stage 4, models update **automatically, triggered by need** rather than the clock. Triggers can be:

| Trigger | Fires when… |
|---|---|
| **Time-based** | a fixed interval elapses (e.g., every 5 minutes) |
| **Performance-based** | model performance plummets |
| **Volume-based** | labeled data grows by some amount (e.g., +5%) |
| **Drift-based** | a major distribution shift is detected |

For these triggers you need a **solid monitoring** solution — and recall from Chapter 8 that the hard part isn't *detecting* changes but deciding *which ones matter*; a noisy monitor will retrain you far more often than necessary. You also need a robust **continual-evaluation** pipeline (covered under *Test in Production*). The **holy grail** is continual learning fused with **edge deployment**: ship a base model on a phone, watch, or drone, and let it adapt on-device with no central server — no server cost, and no data shuttling back and forth, which means better security and privacy.

### How Often to Update Your Models

With the infrastructure in place, *the* question returns: **how often should I retrain?** You can't answer it without first asking a prior question — **how much does fresher data actually help?** The more your model gains from fresh data, the more often you should retrain.

#### Value of data freshness

To measure that gain, train the model on data from **several past time windows** and evaluate every version on **today's** data. For example, with 2020 data: train version A on Jan–Jun, B on Apr–Sep, C on Jun–Nov, then test all three on December. If the quarter-old model is much worse than the month-old one, you know not to wait a quarter to retrain.

With today's faster-moving online content, the value of freshness is likely even higher, and some companies with sophisticated infra retrain **every few minutes**.

#### Model iteration versus data iteration

Beyond *how often*, ask *what kind* of update. Recall the two types: **model iteration** (new feature or architecture) and **data iteration** (same model, fresh data). You should do **both** over time, but resources spent on one are resources not spent on the other — so weigh the return:

- If **data iteration** gives little gain, spend your budget hunting a **better model**.
- If a better architecture needs **100× compute** for a **1%** gain, while refreshing on the last three hours of data needs **1× compute** for the *same* **1%**, clearly **iterate on data**.

No book can tell you which wins for *your* task — you have to **experiment**. The short version: while your infrastructure is immature and updates are slow, retrain **as often as you can**. Once updates are automated and take minutes, the answer hinges entirely on *"how much performance gain would I get from fresher data?"* — so go measure it.

## Test in Production

We've stressed the danger of shipping under-evaluated models. Sufficient evaluation needs **offline** evaluation (Chapter 6) **and** online evaluation. Why isn't offline enough? Consider the two offline tools:

- **Static test splits.** These must stay frozen so you have a trusted, consistent benchmark to compare models on. But a model updated for a *new* distribution shouldn't be judged solely on splits from the *old* one.
- **Backtests.** Testing a model on data from a recent past window (e.g., the last hour) — fresher data is likelier to match the current distribution. But if your pipeline corrupted that last hour, the backtest lies. So keep using a trusted static set as a **sanity check** alongside backtests.

Even so, doing well on the last hour's data is no promise of doing well **tomorrow** — distributions keep shifting. **The only way to know a model works in production is to deploy it.** Hence **test in production** — which sounds terrifying but, done with the right techniques, is mostly safe. We'll cover five: **shadow deployment, A/B testing, canary release, interleaving, and bandits.**

### Shadow Deployment

Shadow deployment is likely the **safest** way to test any model or software update:

1. Deploy the candidate model **in parallel** with the live model.
2. For each incoming request, send it to **both** models — but **only serve the live model's prediction** to the user.
3. **Log** the candidate's predictions for analysis.

Only once the candidate's logged predictions look good do you promote it. Since the candidate never touches a user until it's vetted, the risk is essentially nil. The catch: it's **expensive** — you generate every prediction **twice**, roughly **doubling inference compute**.

### A/B Testing

**A/B testing** compares two variants by splitting real traffic between them and measuring which performs better on predefined metrics. Here, variant A is the live model and variant B the candidate. It's ubiquitous — as of 2017, Microsoft and Google each ran **over 10,000 A/B tests a year** — and it's most ML engineers' first instinct for online evaluation. It works as follows:

1. Deploy the candidate alongside the live model.
2. Route a **percentage of traffic** to the candidate, the rest to the live model. Usually both serve *simultaneously* — but when one model's predictions affect the other's (e.g., ride-sharing prices changing driver/rider supply), you may have to **alternate** them (model A one day, B the next).
3. Monitor predictions and user feedback from both, and test whether the performance difference is **statistically significant**.

Two things matter most. **First, the split must be truly random.** If there's **selection bias** — say users on phones see model A and users on desktops see model B — you can't tell whether A's edge comes from being a better model or from "being on a phone." **Second, you need enough samples** to be confident; computing exactly how many is a deceptively hard statistics problem (consult a dedicated A/B-testing book).

To measure significance, A/B testing uses **statistical hypothesis testing** — the same **two-sample tests** we used in Chapter 8 to detect distribution shift. There, a significant difference meant the distribution had shifted; here, it means we have enough evidence that one model beats the other.

::: callout warning "Statistical significance isn't a guarantee"
Suppose a test says A beats B with **p = 0.05**. That means if you reran the experiment many times, ~95% of runs would favor A — but ~5% would favor B. Even a "significant" result can flip on a rerun. Conversely, an *insignificant* result isn't a failure: if you ran plenty of samples and still found no meaningful gap, the two models are probably about equal, and either is fine to use.
:::

You're not limited to two variants — with multiple candidates you can run **A/B/C** or even **A/B/C/D** tests.

### Canary Release

A **canary release** reduces deployment risk by **gradually rolling out** the new version to a small slice of users before everyone. (The name evokes the canary in a coal mine.) For ML:

1. Deploy the candidate (the **canary**) alongside the live model.
2. Route a portion of traffic to the canary.
3. If its key metrics hold up, **ramp** the traffic up; if they degrade significantly, **abort** and route everything back to the live model.
4. Stop when the canary serves **all** traffic (it has fully replaced the old model) or when it's aborted.

Proposed by Thorsten Joachims in 2002 for search rankings, interleaving lets you compare models by **user preference** rather than group-level core metrics. **Netflix** found it *"reliably identifies the best algorithms with considerably smaller sample size compared to traditional A/B testing"* — a big speed win. ::: callout note "Position bias and team-draft interleaving"
Users click the **top** item far more than the bottom one, so a naive blend would unfairly favor whichever model got the top slot. To keep it fair, **every position must be equally likely to come from A or B.** A clean way is **team-draft interleaving**, mimicking how captains pick players in a pickup game: for each slot, flip a fair coin to choose A or B, and the winner adds its **top not-yet-picked** item. This balances the positions across many sessions so clicks reflect real preference, not layout.
:::

### Bandits

**Bandit** algorithms come from gambling. A casino has many slot machines (each a *"one-armed bandit"*) with unknown payouts; a **multi-armed bandit** algorithm decides how to play them over time to discover the best while **maximizing your winnings along the way**. It balances **exploitation** (play the machine that's paid best so far) against **exploration** (try others that might pay even more).

Map this onto model evaluation: each candidate model is a slot machine whose payout — its prediction accuracy — you don't yet know. A bandit decides how to **route each request** so as to find the best model *while* serving users as accurately as possible. The contrast with A/B testing is sharp:

| | **A/B testing** | **Bandits** |
|---|---|---|
| **State** | **Stateless** — route blindly, ignore current performance | **Stateful** — must know each model's current payoff before routing |
| **Traffic to a worse model** | Keeps sending it the full split for the whole trial | Quickly shifts traffic *away* from losers |
| **Data efficiency** | Needs many samples | **Far** more efficient — often optimal |
| **Works with batch prediction?** | Yes | No — needs **online** predictions |
| **Implementation** | Easy | Hard — must compute and track payoffs |

Bandits need three things: models that can make **online predictions**; **short feedback loops** (e.g., a click instantly signals a good recommendation) so payoffs update quickly; and a **mechanism** to gather feedback, track each model's performance, and route accordingly. The reward is dramatic data efficiency: in a Google experiment, **A/B testing needed over 630,000 samples** for 95% confidence, while a simple bandit (**Thompson Sampling**) flagged a 5% winner with **fewer than 12,000 samples**. The price is complexity — tracking payoffs is hard, so bandits remain rare outside big tech.

::: callout note "A taste of bandit algorithms"
- **ε-greedy** — the simplest. Most of the time (say 90%) route to the current best model; the rest of the time, route to a random one to keep exploring.
- **Thompson Sampling** — pick each model with the probability that it is *the best* given current evidence.
- **Upper Confidence Bound (UCB)** — pick the model with the highest *upper* confidence bound: "optimism in the face of uncertainty," handing an **exploration bonus** to models it's still unsure about.
:::

#### Contextual bandits

Model-evaluation bandits find the payout of each **model**. **Contextual bandits** find the payout of each **action** — in recommendations/ads, an *action* is an item to show and the *payout* is how likely the user clicks it. (Confusingly, some people call model-evaluation bandits "contextual bandits" too; this book reserves the term for action-level exploration.)

Imagine 1,000 items but only 10 slots per page. You only learn about the 10 you *show* — that's the **partial-feedback** (or **bandit-feedback**) problem; you can view contextual bandits as **classification with bandit feedback**. The trap: if you only ever show the current top items, never-shown items keep zero value forever and you can't discover hidden gems. Contextual bandits **balance** showing items users will like against showing items you need feedback on — the same explore–exploit trade-off from reinforcement learning, which is why they're called **"one-shot" RL** (you get feedback immediately after one action, instead of after a long sequence). They significantly boost model performance (per Twitter and Google reports), but are **even harder** to implement than model bandits because the exploration strategy depends on the model's architecture, making it less reusable across use cases.

::: callout key "Evaluation is about *who* tests, not just *what* tests"
A good evaluation pipeline isn't only about which tests to run — it's about **who runs them**. In ML, the people who *built* the model usually also *evaluate* it, and they tend to test ad hoc with their favorite checks. That's doubly risky: it's **biased** (they know the model's quirks and won't use it like real users do) and **inconsistent** (one data scientist declares A better, another disagrees). The fix mirrors software CI/CD: define **clear, automated evaluation pipelines** — which tests, in what order, and the thresholds a model must clear to be promoted — kicked off on every update, with results reported and reviewed.
:::

## Summary

This chapter tackled one of the most exciting yet under-explored topics in ML: how to **continually update models in production** to keep pace with shifting data. We saw that continual learning is really about the **manner** of retraining — **stateless** (from scratch) vs **stateful** (continue from a checkpoint) — and is fundamentally an **infrastructure** problem, not an exotic algorithm. We walked the **four-stage** climb from manual, ad-hoc retraining up to automated, trigger-driven continual learning, and the three challenges along the way: getting **fresh data**, **evaluating** updates safely, and the **algorithmic** awkwardness of matrix- and tree-based models.

We then confronted the question that haunts every ML engineer — *"how often should I retrain?"* — and reframed it around the **value of data freshness** and the trade-off between **model iteration** and **data iteration**: don't guess, **measure**.

Finally, because a retrained model can't be trusted on a frozen test set, we covered **test in production**: **shadow deployment** (safest, but doubles compute), **A/B testing** (the default — randomize and check significance), **canary release** (ramp gradually, abort on trouble), **interleaving** (blend two models' items and read preference, with far fewer samples), and **bandits** (explore–exploit routing that's far more data-efficient but harder to build). Like online prediction, the *evaluation* side of continual learning leans on a **mature streaming infrastructure** — and while streaming used to be hard and costly, tooling has matured fast. Pulling all of this together requires the data/ML team and the **platform team** to work hand in hand — which is exactly where the next chapter on ML infrastructure picks up.

## Key takeaways

::: takeaways
- **Continual learning is about *how* you retrain, not *how often*.** Almost nobody updates per-sample (catastrophic forgetting, wasted compute); real systems update in **micro-batches** using a **champion/challenger** pattern, and only promote a challenger that proves better.
- **Stateful (incremental) training** continues from the last checkpoint on *new* data — needing far less data and compute than **stateless** from-scratch retraining (Grubhub: 45× cheaper, +20% purchase-through), and it can even let you avoid storing data.
- Continual learning combats **sudden distribution shifts**, conquers the **(continuous) cold-start problem**, and handles **rare/seasonal events** like Black Friday. Reframe "why continual learning?" as **"why not?"** — it's a superset of batch learning.
- The three challenges are **fresh-data access** (pull from real-time transports, not slow warehouses; labeling is often the real bottleneck), **evaluation** (more updates = more failure chances; needs test in production), and **algorithms** (matrix/tree models resist fast incremental updates).
- Maturity climbs through **four stages**: manual stateless → automated retraining → automated **stateful** → trigger-driven **continual learning** (time/performance/volume/drift triggers; the holy grail is on-device edge adaptation).
- To set retraining frequency, **measure the value of data freshness** (train on sliding past windows, test on recent data) and weigh **model iteration vs data iteration** by their compute-per-gain.
- **Test in production** safely with **shadow deployment** (safest, 2× compute), **A/B testing** (randomize, check significance — but significance can still flip), **canary release** (ramp, abort on regression), **interleaving** (blend lists, read preference, fewer samples), and **bandits** (explore–exploit, very data-efficient, hard to build).
- A good evaluation process is about **who** runs the tests as much as **what** tests run — automate the pipeline like CI/CD instead of leaving it to ad-hoc, biased, self-grading.
:::

```flashcards
Q: Why don't most companies do true per-sample online learning, and what do they do instead?
A: Per-sample updates cause **catastrophic forgetting** in neural nets and waste batch-oriented hardware. Instead they update in **micro-batches** (e.g., every 512–1,024 examples).
---
Q: What is the champion/challenger pattern in continual learning?
A: The live model is the **champion**; an updated **clone** (the **challenger**) trains on new data in parallel. You promote the challenger only if it proves better — you never mutate the live model in place.
---
Q: Stateless retraining vs stateful training — what's the difference, and why is stateful cheaper?
A: **Stateless** retrains from scratch (needs a long data window, must store data). **Stateful** continues from the last checkpoint on only the newest data (fine-tuning/incremental) — far less data and compute, and a sample is used once so you may not need to store it.
---
Q: What is the continuous cold-start problem, and how does it differ from the classic cold-start problem?
A: Classic cold-start is serving a **brand-new user** with no history. **Continuous** cold-start generalizes it to **existing** users too — when they switch devices, aren't logged in, or visit so rarely their history is stale.
---
Q: Name the three main challenges of continual learning.
A: **Fresh-data access** (warehouse/labeling speed), **evaluation** (safely vetting frequent updates), and **algorithms** (matrix-based and tree-based models are hard to update incrementally).
---
Q: List the four stages of continual learning.
A: (1) Manual stateless retraining, (2) automated retraining, (3) automated **stateful** training, (4) **continual learning** (updates triggered by time/performance/volume/drift).
---
Q: How do you measure the "value of data freshness"?
A: Train the model on several **sliding past time windows** and evaluate every version on the **same recent data**. The performance gap shows how much fresher data is worth and how often to retrain.
---
Q: Why are bandits more data-efficient than A/B testing, and what's the catch?
A: Bandits **shift traffic toward better models** as evidence arrives (explore–exploit), so they need far fewer samples (e.g., ~12k vs ~630k). The catch: they're **stateful** and need online predictions, short feedback loops, and payoff tracking — much harder to build.
```
