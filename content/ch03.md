The rise of machine learning is inseparable from the rise of **big data**. Even without ML, large data systems are genuinely hard — a thicket of acronyms, half-standards, and tools that come and go faster than anyone can keep up. Peer into the data stack of two different tech companies and it can look like each one invented its own universe.

This chapter hands you a steady patch of ground to stand on. Before a model can learn anything, *data* has to be collected, formatted, structured, stored, retrieved, moved between processes, and processed — and the choices you make at each step quietly shape what your ML system can and cannot do later. We'll work outward from the rawest question (where does data even come from?) to the most operational one (how do you process a never-ending stream of it in real time?).

## Data Sources

An ML system can draw on data from many places, each with its own quirks, uses, and processing needs. Knowing where your data comes from is the first step to using it well.

**User input data** is data people enter directly — text, images, video, uploaded files, form fields. The iron law here: *if it is even remotely possible for a user to input the wrong thing, someone will.* Text comes in too long or too short; a number field gets letters; a file upload arrives in an unexpected format. So user input demands **heavy-duty validation and processing**. It also arrives with an audience that has zero patience — people expect a response *immediately* — so user input tends to need **fast processing** too.

**System-generated data** is produced by the components of your system: logs and outputs such as model predictions.

- **Logs** record state and significant events — memory usage, number of running instances, services called, packages used, the outcomes of big batch and training jobs. Their purpose is **visibility**: you rarely read them, but they are indispensable when something is on fire. Because they're machine-generated, logs are far less likely to be malformatted than user input, and most can be processed **periodically** (hourly, even daily) rather than instantly — though you may still want fast processing to *alert* on anything catastrophic.
- Because debugging ML is hard, the common practice is to **log everything**, which makes log volume explode. That creates two problems: signal gets **lost in the noise** (tools like Logstash, Datadog, and Logz.io — many ML-powered — help here), and the sheer volume must be **stored** somewhere. Luckily you usually keep logs only as long as they're useful, and infrequently-accessed logs can live in cheap, low-access storage.
- The system also records **user behavior** — clicks, scrolls, zooms, a dismissed pop-up, an unusually long dwell on a page. Even though the *system* generates it, this is still **user data** and may fall under privacy regulation.

**Internal databases** are generated by a company's services and enterprise apps to manage assets — inventory, customer relationships, users, and more. ML models tap them directly. When you search "frozen" on Amazon, an ML model first disambiguates your intent (frozen *food* or Disney's *Frozen*?), then the system checks **internal databases** for product availability before ranking and showing results.

Finally, the strange world of **third-party data**. The distinction comes down to *whose* customers the data is about:

| Type | Whose data | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **First-party** | Your own | Data your company collects about its own users/customers |
| **Second-party** | Another company's | Data another company collected on *its* customers and makes available to you (usually paid) |
| **Third-party** | The general public | Data a vendor collects on people who are not its direct customers, then sells |

Smartphones once made third-party collection easy: every phone carried a unique advertiser ID — Apple's **IDFA** or Android's **AAID** — that aggregated all activity on the device. Vendors bought and sold all kinds of data (social activity, purchases, browsing, even political leaning), sliced into granular segments like *men, 25–34, in tech, in the Bay Area*, and sold it cleaned and ready — a boon for recommender systems.

::: callout warning "The advertiser-ID era is ending"
As users demand more privacy, companies have curbed advertiser IDs. In early 2021 Apple made **IDFA opt-in**, sharply reducing third-party data on iPhones and pushing companies back toward **first-party data**. Advertisers have fought back with workarounds — e.g., the state-backed CAID **device-fingerprinting** scheme that let apps like TikTok keep tracking iPhone users. The takeaway for ML teams: third-party data is becoming less reliable and less available; lean on data you collect yourself.
:::

## Data Formats

Once you have data you'll want to **store** (or *persist*) it. Because your data arrives from many sources with different **access patterns** — the way a program reads or writes it — storage isn't always straightforward, and for some cases it's costly. The format you pick should reflect how the data will be used later. A few questions worth asking up front:

- How do I store **multimodal** data (a sample with both an image and text)?
- Where do I store data so it's **cheap** yet still **fast** to access?
- How do I store **complex models** so they load and run correctly on different hardware?

The act of turning a data structure or object into something that can be stored or transmitted and rebuilt later is **data serialization**. There are *many* serialization formats; when choosing one, weigh **human readability**, **access patterns**, and **text vs. binary** (which drives file size). A few you'll meet often:

| Format | Binary / Text | Human-readable | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **JSON** | Text | Yes | Everywhere |
| **CSV** | Text | Yes | Everywhere |
| **Parquet** | Binary | No | Hadoop, Amazon Redshift |
| **Avro** | Binary (primarily) | No | Hadoop |
| **Protobuf** | Binary (primarily) | No | Google, TensorFlow (TFRecord) |
| **Pickle** | Binary | No | Python, PyTorch serialization |

### JSON

**JSON** (JavaScript Object Notation) is everywhere. Though born in JavaScript, it's **language-independent** — nearly every modern language can read and write it — and it's **human-readable**. Its key–value paradigm is simple yet powerful, handling data across the whole spectrum of structuredness. The same person can be stored as a tidy **structured** object…

```
{ "firstName": "Boatie", "age": 12,
  "address": { "city": "Port Royal", "postalCode": "10021-3100" } }
```

…or as an **unstructured** blob of text inside a single field:

```
{ "text": "Boatie McBoatFace, aged 12, lives at 12 Ocean Drive, Port Royal" }
```

Because JSON is ubiquitous, so is its pain. Once you've committed your JSON to a **schema**, retroactively changing that schema is painful. And because JSON files are **text**, they take up a lot of space — which brings us to the two formats that embody two opposite paradigms: **CSV** and **Parquet**.

### Row-Major Versus Column-Major Format

**CSV is row-major:** consecutive elements *in a row* sit next to each other in memory. **Parquet is column-major:** consecutive elements *in a column* sit next to each other. Because modern CPUs read **sequential** memory far faster than scattered memory, the storage order decides which access pattern is cheap.

In ML we usually treat each **example as a row** and each **feature as a column**. So **CSV (row-major) is better for accessing examples** — "give me every example collected today" — while **Parquet (column-major) is better for accessing features** — "give me the timestamp of every example."

The column advantage is dramatic at width. Imagine ride-sharing data with **1,000 features** where you want just **4** (time, location, distance, price). With a column-major format you read exactly those four columns. With a row-major format, you must read in **all** columns and then filter — and even if you know the row sizes, jumping around memory defeats the CPU cache. Conversely, **row-major formats write faster**: appending one new example to row-major storage is cheap, which matters when examples stream in one at a time. In short — **column-major for heavy column reads, row-major for heavy writes.**

::: callout note "pandas is columnar; NumPy is row-major by default"
A subtle source of slowness: **pandas** is built around the **DataFrame** (inspired by R), which is **column-major**. People arriving from **NumPy** — whose `ndarray` is **row-major by default** — instinctively iterate a DataFrame *by row* and find it crawling. Chip's benchmark: iterating a DataFrame **by column** took **0.07 s**, but iterating the *same* DataFrame **by row** took **2.41 s**. Convert that DataFrame to a NumPy `ndarray` and row access becomes fast again. Match your access pattern to the layout.
:::

::: callout warning "CSV: popular, but a flawed format"
CSV is used here because everyone recognizes it — but it's not a great format. It serializes non-text characters poorly: a float like `0.12345678901232323` can be silently rounded to `0.12345678901`, losing precision. CSV draws regular complaints on Stack Overflow and Hacker News for exactly this. For real numeric data, prefer a binary format like Parquet.
:::

### Text Versus Binary Format

CSV and JSON are **text** files — plain text, usually human-readable. **Parquet is binary.** "Binary" is the catchall for everything non-text: files of raw 0s and 1s that only a program knowing the exact byte layout can interpret. Open a text file in your editor and you read words; open a binary file and you see blocks of hex.

**Binary files are more compact.** Store the number `1000000` as text and it needs **7 characters ≈ 7 bytes**; store it as a binary `int32` and it needs only **4 bytes**. The savings compound at scale: Chip converted a CSV of 17,654 rows × 10 columns (`interviews.csv`) to Parquet and the file shrank from **14 MB to 6 MB**. AWS makes the same point — Parquet is "up to 2× faster to unload and consumes up to 6× less storage in S3" than text.

| Format | Text / Binary | Human-readable | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **JSON** | Text | Yes | Config, APIs, flexible/nested records — everywhere |
| **CSV** | Text | Yes | Quick interchange, spreadsheets, simple tables |
| **Parquet** | Binary | No | Analytics & ML: columnar, compressed, big tables |

## Data Models

A **data model** describes how data is represented — and that choice shapes both how you build your system *and which problems it can solve*. A car could be modeled by **make, model, year, color, price** (handy for a buyer) or by **owner, license plate, history of registered addresses** (handy for a police officer). Same car, different model, different superpowers. We'll look at two families that seem opposite but are quietly converging: **relational** and **NoSQL**.

### Relational Model

One of computer science's most durable ideas, the **relational model** was invented by **Edgar F. Codd in 1970** and is, if anything, *more* popular today. Data is organized into **relations** (each a set of tuples); the familiar visual is a **table** where each **row is a tuple**. Relations are **unordered** — shuffle the rows or the columns and it's still the same relation. Relational data is usually stored in formats like CSV or Parquet.

It's often desirable to **normalize** relations. Normalization follows *normal forms* (1NF, 2NF, …) and its job is to **reduce redundancy and improve integrity**. Take a `Book` table where many rows repeat the same publisher details:

| Title | Author | Format | Publisher | Country | Price |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Harry Potter | J.K. Rowling | Paperback | Banana Press | UK | \$20 |
| Harry Potter | J.K. Rowling | E-book | Banana Press | UK | \$10 |
| The Hobbit | J.R.R. Tolkien | Paperback | Banana Press | UK | \$30 |

If "Banana Press" is renamed, you must hunt down and update **every** row that mentions it. Split the publisher out into its own relation and link by **Publisher ID**, and now a publisher change touches **one row**:

| Title | Author | Format | Publisher ID | Price |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Harry Potter | J.K. Rowling | Paperback | 1 | \$20 |
| The Hobbit | J.R.R. Tolkien | Paperback | 1 | \$30 |

| Publisher ID | Publisher | Country |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Banana Press | UK |
| 2 | Guava Press | US |

The downside: your data is now **spread across relations**, and stitching it back together with a **join** can be expensive for large tables.

Databases built on this model are **relational databases**, and you retrieve data with a **query language** — overwhelmingly **SQL**. The single most important thing about SQL is that it's a **declarative** language, unlike imperative Python.

::: callout key "Declarative vs. imperative"
**Imperative** (Python): you specify the **steps**, and the computer runs them. **Declarative** (SQL): you specify the **result you want** — which tables, which conditions, which joins/sorts/aggregations — and the computer figures out *how* to get it. You say *what*, not *how*. With enough features SQL is even **Turing-complete** (it can express any computation in theory), though in practice some queries are brutal to write or to run.
:::

Deciding *how* to execute an arbitrary query is the hard part — the job of the **query optimizer**, which examines many possible execution plans and picks the fastest. Query optimization is one of the most challenging problems in databases (normalization makes it harder by scattering data across relations), and there's active research on using **ML to learn better optimizers**. The good news: you generally need **one** optimizer and every application benefits from it.

::: callout note "From declarative data to declarative ML"
Inspired by SQL's success, some hope for **declarative ML**: you declare the feature schema and the task, and the system finds the best model — no hand-written training code. Frameworks like Uber's **Ludwig** and **H2O AutoML** point this way. But declarative ML abstracts away **model development**, which (as the next six chapters argue) is often the *easy* part. The genuinely hard problems — feature engineering, data processing, evaluation, **data-shift detection**, continual learning — remain unsolved by it.
:::

### NoSQL

The relational model generalizes remarkably well, but it can be restrictive: it demands a **strict schema**, and schema management is painful (in a 2014 Couchbase survey, schema frustration was the **#1 reason** teams adopted a non-relational database). The backlash is **NoSQL** — originally a meetup hashtag, later reinterpreted as **"Not Only SQL,"** since many NoSQL systems also support relations. Two major non-relational models matter here.

#### Document Model

The **document model** is built around the **document** — usually one continuous string encoded as JSON, XML, or binary **BSON**, with a **unique key** to retrieve it. A **collection** of documents is loosely like a table, and a document like a row — but far more flexible: every row in a table must share one schema, whereas **documents in the same collection can have completely different schemas**. The book example earlier collapses into one self-contained document per book:

```
{ "Title": "Harry Potter", "Publisher": "Banana Press", "Country": "UK",
  "Sold as": [ {"Format": "Paperback", "Price": "$20"},
               {"Format": "E-book", "Price": "$10"} ] }
```

Because it enforces no schema, the document model is often called **schemaless** — which is **misleading**. The data *will* be read later, and the reader still assumes *some* structure. The model simply **shifts the responsibility** for assuming structure from the code that **writes** the data to the code that **reads** it.

Its big strength is **locality**: everything about a book lives in one document, so one read returns it all (versus joining `Book`, `Publisher`, and `Format` tables). Its weakness is **joins** — finding "all books under \$25" means reading **every** document, extracting each price, and filtering. Because the two models have complementary strengths, it's common to use **both** in one system; PostgreSQL and MySQL now support each.

#### Graph Model

The **graph model** is built around the **graph** — **nodes** plus **edges**, where edges *are* the relationships. In a graph database the **relationships are the priority** (in a document database, the document content is). Because relationships are modeled **explicitly**, retrieving data *by relationship* is fast.

Picture a small social graph with nodes of many types — `person`, `city`, `country`, `company`. To find **everyone born in the USA**, start at the `USA` node and traverse `within` and `born_in` edges to every `person`. The catch that makes this *hard* for other models: there's an **unknown number of hops** between country and person — three hops from one person to the USA, two from another — and neither SQL nor a document store handles a variable-length traversal gracefully. The lesson recurs all chapter: **a query that's trivial in one data model can be miserable in another, so pick the model that fits your queries.**

### Structured Versus Unstructured Data

**Structured data** follows a predefined **schema** — say, `name` is a string ≤ 50 chars and `age` is an 8-bit integer in 0–200. Structure makes analysis trivial (average age = pull every `age` and average it), but it has a cost: **commit to a schema and any change forces a retroactive update** of all existing data, often spawning mysterious bugs.

::: callout example "The 0-year-old customers"
A colleague's schema started replacing every **null age** with **0**. Their ML model promptly concluded that a wave of transactions had been made by **people aged 0**. Schema changes don't just inconvenience engineers — they silently corrupt the signal a model learns from. (In that case, swapping nulls for `-1` fixed it.)
:::

**Unstructured data** adheres to no predefined schema. It's usually text but can be numbers, dates, images, or audio — a log file is unstructured. It may still carry **intrinsic patterns** (each line is "word, number"), but nothing *guarantees* every line conforms; you can always append a row that breaks the pattern. Unstructured data buys **flexible storage and fast arrival**: convert anything to bytestrings and store it together, no schema gatekeeper.

The repositories that hold them have names: a **data warehouse** stores **structured** data (processed and ready to use), and a **data lake** stores **unstructured/raw** data (typically before processing).

| | **Structured data** | **Unstructured data** |
|---|---|---|
| **Schema** | Clearly defined up front | None required |
| **Strength** | Easy to search & analyze | Fast arrival |
| **Source flexibility** | Only data fitting the schema | Data from *any* source |
| **Schema changes** | Cause a lot of trouble | No worries yet — the burden shifts to readers |
| **Stored in** | **Data warehouse** | **Data lake** |

::: callout key "The real dividing line"
Structured vs. unstructured is **fluid**. The honest question isn't "does it have a schema?" but **"who assumes the structure?"** Structured data → the **writer** assumes it. Unstructured data → the **reader** assumes it. That single reframe explains warehouses vs. lakes and relational vs. document models alike.
:::

## Data Storage Engines and Processing

Formats and models define the **interface** for storing and retrieving data. **Storage engines** (a.k.a. **databases**) are the **implementation** — how data is actually stored and fetched on machines. Databases are typically optimized for one of two very different workloads.

### Transactional and Analytical Processing

A **transaction** once meant buying or selling; digitally it means *any* action — a tweet, a ride request, a model upload, a video view. These are **inserted** as they happen, occasionally **updated**, and **deleted** when obsolete. This is **online transaction processing (OLTP)**. Because transactions involve waiting users, they need **low latency** and **high availability** — if the system can't process a transaction, it simply doesn't go through.

Transactional databases are built for those demands, and people associate them with **ACID**:

| Property | Guarantee | Ride-sharing example |
|---|---|---|
| **Atomicity** | All steps in a transaction succeed, or none do | If payment fails, don't assign a driver |
| **Consistency** | Every transaction obeys predefined rules | A transaction must come from a valid user |
| **Isolation** | Concurrent transactions act as if run one at a time | Two users can't book the same driver at once |
| **Durability** | A committed transaction survives system failure | Phone dies after ordering — the ride still comes |

ACID isn't mandatory; some find it too restrictive. Systems that relax it are sometimes called **BASE** (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventual consistency) — even vaguer than ACID. Because each transaction is handled as its own unit, transactional databases are usually **row-major**, which makes them poor at questions like *"average ride price in September in San Francisco?"* — that's an **analytical** query, aggregating a column across many rows. **Analytical databases** exist for exactly that, and the workload is **online analytical processing (OLAP)**.

::: callout warning "OLTP and OLAP are getting outdated"
Three shifts (per Google Trends, ~2021) have blurred these terms:

1. **The wall is falling.** The split existed because no single database could do both well — but now **CockroachDB** (transactional) handles analytical queries, and **Apache Iceberg** and **DuckDB** (analytical) handle transactional ones.
2. **Storage and compute are decoupling.** Classically, *how data is stored* dictated *how it's processed*, often forcing the same data into multiple databases. The last decade's big idea — adopted by **BigQuery, Snowflake, IBM, Teradata** — is to **store data once** and put an optimizable **processing layer** on top.
3. **"Online" is overloaded.** It once meant "on the internet," then "in production," and now often denotes **processing speed**: **online** (instantly available), **nearline** (available quickly, no human needed), **offline** (needs human intervention).
:::

### ETL: Extract, Transform, Load

When relational data ruled, data extracted from sources was **transformed into the desired shape before** being **loaded** into a database or warehouse — **ETL** (Extract, Transform, Load). It predates ML, remains central, and is the backbone of the data layer at countless organizations.

- **Extract** — pull data from every source, **validate** it, and **reject** what doesn't meet requirements (notifying the source if needed). Getting this first step right saves enormous pain downstream.
- **Transform** — the meaty part: **join** sources, **clean**, standardize value ranges (`Male/Female` vs. `M/F` vs. `1/2`), transpose, deduplicate, sort, aggregate, derive new features, validate again.
- **Load** — decide **how** and **how often** to load the transformed data into the destination (a file, database, or warehouse).

When the internet exploded, data outgrew tidy schemas, so some teams flipped the order: **store everything raw in a data lake first, process later**. Loading before transforming is **ELT** (Extract, **Load**, Transform), and it buys **fast arrival** since little processing happens before storage.

### Data Passing Through Databases

The simplest mode: process A **writes** to a database and process B **reads** from it. But it breaks down for two reasons. First, both processes must **share the same database** — often infeasible, especially across companies. Second, database reads/writes are **slow**, ruling this out for **strict-latency** applications — i.e., almost anything consumer-facing.

### Data Passing Through Services

Instead, pass data **directly over a network**: A sends B a **request** specifying what it needs, and B returns it. Because communication happens through requests, this is **request-driven**, and it's tightly bound to **service-oriented architecture** — a **service** is a process reachable remotely. When you split an application's components into independent services (each developed, tested, and deployed on its own), you get a **microservice architecture**.

::: callout example "Three services in a ride-sharing app"
Lyft runs *hundreds* of services; simplified to three:
- **Driver management** — predicts available drivers in an area next minute.
- **Ride management** — predicts requested rides in an area next minute.
- **Price optimization** — predicts each ride's optimal price (low enough for riders, high enough for drivers and profit).

Price depends on **supply and demand**, so price optimization **requests** data from the other two on every ride. This is the canonical request-driven dependency.
:::

The two dominant request styles are **REST** (Representational State Transfer) and **RPC** (Remote Procedure Call). The key difference: **REST** was designed for requests **over networks** and dominates **public APIs**; **RPC** tries to make a remote call **look like a local function call** and is favored **between services owned by one organization**, often in the same data center. Note that **REST ≠ HTTP** — HTTP is just one implementation of REST (implementations are called **RESTful**).

### Data Passing Through Real-Time Transport

Request-driven passing strains as services multiply. Suppose **all three** services now need each other's data — driver management wants ride counts and predicted prices (to dangle surge incentives), and so on. With direct requests, every service calls the other two, and with hundreds of services this **web of interservice calls** becomes a system-wide bottleneck.

Worse, request-driven passing is **synchronous**: the target must be listening. If price optimization asks a **downed** driver-management service, it retries until timeout; if price optimization itself dies before a reply, the **response is lost**. **One down service can take down everything depending on it.**

The fix is a **broker** that coordinates passing. Each service talks **only to the broker**: when driver management makes a prediction it **broadcasts** it to the broker, and any service wanting that data reads the latest value from the broker. A database *could* broker this, but it's too slow — so we use **in-memory** storage. A **real-time transport** is essentially in-memory storage for passing data among services; a broadcast piece of data is an **event**, which is why this is **event-driven** (the transport is sometimes an **event bus**).

::: callout key "Request-driven vs. event-driven — when to use which"
**Request-driven** (REST/RPC, point-to-point) suits systems that lean more on **logic** than data. **Event-driven** (pub/sub broker) suits **data-heavy** systems, trading synchronous coupling for asynchronous, low-latency, decoupled passing — producers don't know or care who consumes their events.
:::

The two most common real-time transports:

| | **Pub/sub (publish–subscribe)** | **Message queue** |
|---|---|---|
| **How it routes** | Any service **publishes** to a **topic**; any service **subscribed** to that topic reads all its events | An event often has **intended consumers** (then called a **message**); the queue delivers it to the right ones |
| **Producer ↔ consumer** | Producers don't care who consumes | Targeted at specific consumers |
| **Retention** | Often a **retention policy** — events kept N days (e.g., 7), then deleted or moved to permanent storage like S3 | Delivered, then done |
| **Examples** | **Apache Kafka, Amazon Kinesis** | **Apache RocketMQ, RabbitMQ** |

## Batch Processing Versus Stream Processing

Once data lands in a storage engine (database, lake, warehouse) it becomes **historical data**; data still arriving in real-time transports is **streaming data**. They differ on a deep axis — **bounded vs. unbounded** — and demand different processing.

- **Batch processing** runs on **historical (bounded)** data in **periodic jobs** — say, a daily job computing yesterday's average surge charge. Decades of research gave us distributed batch engines like **MapReduce** and **Spark**.
- **Stream processing** runs on **streaming (unbounded)** data. It can run on short cycles (every five minutes, not every day) or fire **on demand** — e.g., the instant a user requests a ride, process the stream to see which drivers are free right now. Done right it gives **low latency**, since data is processed as it's generated, with no detour through a database first.

A common belief is that streaming is *less efficient* than batch because you can't lean on MapReduce/Spark. Not necessarily: engines like **Apache Flink** are proven highly scalable and fully distributed (parallel computation), and stream processing's real strength is **stateful computation**. Computing 30-day trial engagement as a daily *batch* job recomputes the full 30 days every day; a *stream* processor computes only the **new** data each day and **joins** it to the prior result — no redundancy.

This split maps directly onto **features**:

| | **Batch processing** | **Stream processing** |
|---|---|---|
| **Data** | Historical, **bounded** | Streaming, **unbounded** |
| **Cadence** | Periodic (e.g., daily) | Continuous / on-demand (e.g., every few min) |
| **Engines** | MapReduce, Spark | Apache Flink, KSQL, Spark Streaming |
| **Best at** | Heavy throughput on stable data | Low latency, **stateful** computation |
| **Produces** | **Static features** (slow-changing — a driver's rating) | **Dynamic features** (fast-changing — drivers free *now*, rides in the last minute) |

::: callout note "You usually need both"
Most real systems need **both** kinds of features and infrastructure that processes batch and streaming data and **joins** them to feed the model (Chapter 7 goes deeper). For the streaming side, Kafka's built-in compute handles only simple cases; serious feature extraction — hundreds or thousands of features in fraud detection or credit scoring, with joins and aggregations across dimensions — needs a real **stream processing engine** like **Apache Flink** or **KSQL** (both offer a friendly SQL abstraction). It's easier to make a stream processor do batch than the reverse, which is why Flink's maintainers argue **batch is a special case of streaming.**
:::

## Key takeaways

::: takeaways
- **Data sources** vary in trust and tempo: **user input** needs the heaviest validation and fast processing; **system-generated** logs can be processed periodically; **internal databases** feed models directly; **third-party** data is shrinking as privacy rules tighten.
- **Format matters.** Row-major (**CSV**) is fast for whole-example reads and writes; column-major (**Parquet**) is fast for reading a few features across many examples. **pandas is columnar; NumPy is row-major by default** — match your access pattern. **Binary** beats text on size (14 MB CSV → 6 MB Parquet).
- Three **data models**: **relational** (normalized tables, declarative SQL), **document** (self-contained JSON, great locality, weak joins), and **graph** (nodes/edges, fast relationship traversal). A query trivial in one is often painful in another.
- **Structured vs. unstructured** comes down to **who assumes the structure** — the **writer** (warehouse) or the **reader** (lake).
- **OLTP** (row-oriented, low-latency, ACID) vs. **OLAP** (column-oriented, aggregating reads); **decoupling storage from compute** has blurred the line. **ETL** transforms-then-loads into a warehouse; **ELT** loads-then-transforms into a lake; **lakehouses** blend both.
- Three **dataflow modes**: through **databases** (simple, slow), **request-driven services** (REST/RPC, synchronous, microservices), and **event-driven real-time transport** (pub/sub via Kafka/Kinesis, message queues via RabbitMQ — a **broker decouples** producers from consumers).
- **Batch** processing on **bounded** historical data yields **static** features; **stream** processing on **unbounded** streaming data yields **dynamic** features. Most systems need **both**.
:::

## Test your knowledge

```flashcards
Q: Why does user input data require the heaviest validation of any data source?
A: If users *can* enter the wrong thing, they will — text too long/short, letters in number fields, files in the wrong format. So user input needs heavy-duty checking, and (because users are impatient) fast processing too.
---
Q: Row-major (CSV) vs. column-major (Parquet) — which access pattern is fast for each, treating examples as rows and features as columns?
A: Row-major is fast for reading a whole **example** (and for writes/appends). Column-major is fast for reading one **feature** across all examples — e.g., pulling 4 of 1,000 columns without touching the rest.
---
Q: A teammate finds their pandas DataFrame painfully slow to iterate. What's the likely mistake?
A: pandas DataFrames are **column-major**, but they're iterating **by row** (a NumPy habit, since `ndarray` is row-major by default). Iterating by column — or converting to a NumPy array for row access — fixes it.
---
Q: What's the difference between structured and unstructured data, framed by responsibility?
A: It's about **who assumes the structure**. Structured → the **writer** commits to a schema (stored in a data **warehouse**). Unstructured → the **reader** assumes structure at read time (stored in a data **lake**).
---
Q: Name the three modes of dataflow between processes, with a one-line tradeoff for each.
A: (1) Through **databases** — simple but slow and needs a shared DB. (2) **Request-driven services** (REST/RPC) — synchronous, point-to-point, microservices. (3) **Event-driven real-time transport** (Kafka/Kinesis) — async, low-latency, a broker decouples producers from consumers.
---
Q: Batch vs. stream processing — what data does each handle, and what kind of feature does each produce?
A: **Batch** runs periodic jobs over **bounded historical** data → slow-changing **static** features (e.g., a driver's rating). **Stream** runs continuously over **unbounded streaming** data → fast-changing **dynamic** features (e.g., drivers free right now).
---
Q: What does it mean that SQL is declarative, and why does that matter?
A: You specify the **result** you want (tables, conditions, joins) but not **how** to get it; the database's **query optimizer** figures out the fastest execution plan. You write *what*, not *how* — and one good optimizer benefits every application.
---
Q: ETL vs. ELT — what's the difference and where does each load data?
A: **ETL** transforms data into the target schema **before** loading it into a **warehouse**. **ELT** loads raw data **first** (into a **lake**) and transforms later, enabling fast arrival. Lakehouses blend the two.
```
